% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper studies the design of a dynamic indirect mechanism to deter and
dismantle criminal organizations under incomplete information, with application to
kidnapping for ransom in Colombia. The empirical evidence provides the mechanism's prior
and parameters: a vector error correction model characterizes the macroeconomic
long-run trends of kidnapping and interest rates; multinomial logit and competing
hazard models identify costs, durations, outcomes, and heterogeneity across criminal
types. The central theoretical contribution is the Theorem on the Identification of
Rational Types in Unknown Environments, which transforms recurrent separation between
observable signals into posterior identification of the true type. Under consistent
Bayesian updating in the prolonged captivity event, the State's beliefs converge
almost surely to the true type of the captor. The paper also establishes conditional
implementability in Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium when the feasible policy set is
non-empty. The dynamic calibration illustrates the operational viability of the
mechanism and shows how state instruments can accelerate learning and discipline
optimal policy.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} mechanism design; Bayesian learning; competing risks;
kidnapping; dual control; identification.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{JEL Codes:} C73, D82, K42.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Related Literature}
\label{sec:intro}

Observable criminal behavior tends to group distinct types precisely when public policy
needs to discriminate among them. A ransom demand, a period of silence, a partial
proof of survival, or a violent escalation may each be compatible with different
organizational technologies. When a single public signal can be produced by disciplined
insurgent organizations, short-horizon criminal gangs, or fragmented structures, a
purely passive policy faces an informational deadlock: the decision that minimizes
immediate loss may preserve the pooling that prevents identifying the adversary in
the future. Mechanism design provides the architecture to formulate rules that align
private incentives with the State's normative objectives under voluntary participation
and sequential rationality.

The central question is how to design an active screening mechanism when observable
actions lie in pooling regions. The planner is the State; its continuous instruments are
financial interdiction $\alpha_t\in[0,1]$ and operational pressure $\gamma_t\in[0,1]$.
The adversary possesses a private type $\theta_K\in\Theta_K$. The problem therefore
consists not only of choosing between rescue and negotiation, but of calibrating
$(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$ so that the induced law of daily signals is informative about
the latent criminal technology.

This paper sits at the intersection of dynamic mechanism design, the economics of
crime, and active learning. The Revelation Principle and incentive compatibility and
individual rationality constraints provide the foundation of the implementation problem
\parencite{Gibbard1973,Myerson1979IC,Myerson1981,Rochet1987}. The dynamic mechanism
design literature extends this approach to intertemporal environments
\parencite{CourtyLi2000,EsoSzentes2007,PavanSegalToikka2014}, while the dynamic
information acquisition literature shows why an optimal policy must value the effect
of present actions on the quality of future signals \parencite{Zhong2022}. The
economics of crime and kidnapping studies document incentives, durations, and
organizational heterogeneity \parencite{Becker1968,Ehrlich1973,Selten1977Kidnapping,
Mejia2000Secuestro,Detotto2014Ransom,Forest2012Kidnapping,Shortland2019Kidnap}.

The contribution integrates three elements. First, the Mano de Dios--Guadalupe (MDG)
process introduces stochastic implementation with full support, keeping the Bayesian
operator well-defined off the equilibrium path. Second, a competing risks structure
connects duration, mode of termination, and learning. Third, the Theorem on the
Identification of Rational Types in Unknown Environments converts a local condition of
recurrent separation into global posterior identification. The Implementability Theorem
complements this result by establishing the conditions under which the State's rule can
be sustained in Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium without violating $IC^K$, $IR^K$, or
$IR^F$.

\section{Institutional Framework and Stylized Facts}
\label{sec:institutional}

\subsection{Kidnapping as a Concentrated and Heterogeneous Phenomenon}

UNODC records \parencite{unodc_Data} for 145 countries in 2023--2024 count
1,514,546 kidnapping cases and reveal a distributional asymmetry with direct
implications for analysis. The median annual average per country stands at 46 cases,
while the mean reaches 1,153---a mean-to-median ratio of approximately 25:1,
ruling out the existence of a representative country and indicating that the
distribution of national annual averages has a heavy tail. The 75th percentile is 109
cases, meaning three-quarters of countries record volumes below this threshold while
the upper quarter concentrates the dominant share of the world total. This extreme
concentration structure, analogous to income or firm-size distributions, invalidates
interpretations based on average behavior and demands treating institutional, legal,
and criminological heterogeneity across countries as a structural attribute of the
phenomenon---motivating the heterogeneous-types approach adopted here.

The world map in Figure~\ref{fig:figure_1} shows that several developed countries
rank among the highest in absolute kidnapping numbers, in some cases exceeding
countries typically associated with lower living standards, high income inequality,
elevated poverty, and prolonged armed conflict, such as India, Pakistan, Afghanistan,
Somalia, Haiti, Ecuador, Venezuela, and Colombia. This configuration does not
necessarily reflect greater physical risk for the general population; rather, it is
driven by substantive differences in the legal definition of kidnapping, institutional
capacity for data recording, and propensity to report crimes, as documented in
\textcite{UNODC2006Manual} and \textcite{UNODC2016ICCS}. In developed jurisdictions,
official statistics encompass behaviors such as parental abduction, unlawful
deprivation of liberty in family contexts, and certain forms of criminal exploitation,
substantially expanding the statistical universe relative to countries where only
ransom kidnappings are recorded.

In Colombia, between 1964 and 2025, the rate of ransom and non-extortionary kidnapping
per 100,000 inhabitants follows a non-linear trajectory reflecting the interaction of
armed conflict dynamics, illicit economies, economic activity, and state capacity
(Figure~\ref{fig:figure_1}). From the mid-1960s through the late 1970s, the rate
remained low and relatively stable at approximately 0.2--0.5 kidnappings per 100,000
inhabitants, associated with marginal forms of violence and the incipient stage of
insurgent groups. A structural break emerged in the late 1970s, with the rate exceeding
4 kidnappings per 100,000 inhabitants in the early 1980s. Through the 1980s and 1990s,
kidnapping exhibited a growth pattern with volatility, associated with the consolidation
of guerrilla groups, narco-trafficking expansion, and the use of kidnapping as a
financing and political coercion mechanism \parencite{pinto2004,Zarate2007}. This
process culminated in a historical peak near 9.6 kidnappings per 100,000 inhabitants
around 2000, coinciding with weakened state control and armed conflict escalation.
From 2002 onward, a persistent reversal occurred, with a sustained decline reaching
levels below 1 per 100,000 during the 2010s and historical lows after the 2016 Peace
Agreement. A slight recent increase, still far below past critical levels, suggests
a reconfiguration associated with fragmentation of armed actors.

In absolute terms (Figure~\ref{figure_2}), annual kidnapping records remained below
100 cases through the late 1970s. A structural break in the early 1980s pushed
annual counts above 1,000. During the 1980s--1990s, volume grew sustainedly but
with volatility, reaching a historical peak of approximately 3,700 cases around
1999--2002 amid conflict expansion and illicit economies. From 2002 a persistent
reduction followed, with annual records below 500 during the 2010s. Spatially
(Figure~\ref{figure_2}b), kidnappings occurred across most of the territory but were
heavily concentrated: Bogot\'a D.C.\ (1,437 cases), Cali (784), and Medell\'in (652)
lead, followed by intermediate cities and strategic municipalities such as Valledupar
(530), Argelia (452), Villavicencio (436), Aguachica (436), Oca\~na (348),
Bucaramanga (304), and Santa Marta (291). This spatial concentration, consistent with
the crime-triangle framework of \textcite{Pires2014}, reflects differentiated criminal
opportunities, strategic corridors, and persistent state capacity limitations rather
than a uniform distribution across the territory.

Kidnappings of foreigners followed a parallel trajectory to total cases, though at
smaller scale (Figure~\ref{fig:figure_3}). Annual cases were near zero through the
late 1970s; peaks exceeding 40 cases per year (and a maximum of approximately 95)
occurred during the 1990s and early 2000s. By nationality, Venezuelans represent the
largest number of recorded cases (41), followed by Ecuadorians (20), Spaniards (15),
and US citizens (12), reflecting a rationality of opportunity and strategic calculation
configured by conflict intensity and the perceived ransom-paying capacity and
international visibility of specific nationalities.

The establishment of the GAULA units (Grupos de Acci\'on Unificados para la
Libertad Personal), enacted by \textcite{ley282_1996}, marked a strategic transition
from fragmented anti-kidnapping units toward a unified action doctrine, consolidated
with the creation of the Police Anti-Kidnapping Directorate \parencite{decreto864_1998}.
Their deployment, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:maps_total_foreigners}, prioritized
primary centers of criminal activity rather than arbitrary administrative distribution.
A national network of 34 units covered strategic urban nodes; in several complex
jurisdictions (Medell\'in, Valledupar, Cartagena), dual institutional presence was
maintained. However, municipalities with high kidnapping intensity such as Argelia,
Buenaventura, and Oca\~na lack local GAULA units, highlighting a disconnection between
reactive state capacity and crime persistence. The distribution of 3,949 arrests in
ransom kidnapping episodes (2010--2025) reveals that a substantial fraction concentrates
in cities with GAULA presence: Bogot\'a (15.93\%), Medell\'in (9.29\%), Cali (7.39\%),
Villavicencio (2.38\%), and C\'ucuta (2.36\%) lead, consistent with greater operational,
investigative, and prosecutorial capacity at these metropolitan centers.

Kidnapping in Colombia has been closely linked to the evolution of the internal armed
conflict and the rise and consolidation of illegal armed actors. Figure~\ref{fig:figure_5}
provides an integrated synthesis of this dynamic across successive presidential
administrations, connecting changes in kidnapping patterns to distinct conflict phases
and the main norms and institutional milestones shaping the state response. Through
the 1960s--1970s, kidnapping appeared as a low-intensity, predominantly rural phenomenon
consistent with the early stages of insurgent formation and a still-limited state
capacity. A sustained escalation phase became evident beginning with the Turbay Ayala
administration and more clearly under Betancur and Barco, coinciding with guerrilla
expansion, narco-trafficking actors, and growing self-defense groups. This period also
saw targeted normative responses including \textcite{Decreto1923_1978} and subsequent
decrees. The masification phase under Gaviria, Samper, and especially Pastrana saw
kidnapping reach its highest levels---exceeding 13,000 cases in 1998--2002---coinciding
with the 1991 Constitution, cartel pressure, paramilitary consolidation, and the
Caugu\'an demilitarized zone, alongside \textcite{ley40_1993} and \textcite{ley282_1996}
establishing the National Anti-Kidnapping Statute and GAULA. From 2002 onward, the
Uribe administrations' security policy, strengthened military and police capacity, and
legal reforms including \textcite{ley733_2002}, \textcite{ley890_2004}, and
\textcite{ley1200_2008}---a penal tightening framework substantially raising the costs
of kidnapping---drove a containment phase. The Santos administrations saw a readjustment
phase at low kidnapping levels in the context of the peace process and the signing of the
Final Peace Agreement with the FARC, supported by instruments such as \textcite{ley1957_2019},
which introduced conditional sentence reductions for kidnapping in cases where perpetrators
submitted to the JEP. Under Petro, a moderate resurgence is observed, associated with
the fragmentation of armed actors, the re-emergence of dissident groups, and evolving
conflict dynamics within the regulatory environment shaped by \textcite{ley2272_2022}.

\subsection{Macroeconomic Analysis: Stochastic Trend}
\label{subsec:macro}

Under Colombian criminal law, kidnapping is classified into two types: ransom
kidnapping and non-extortionary kidnapping. Figure~\ref{fig:figure_6} shows the
historical trajectory of these crimes from 1964 to 2025. The aggregate kidnapping
volume has been driven largely by the dynamics of the extortionary modality, a
correlation especially pronounced during the 1996--2002 peak reflecting the
instrumental use of kidnapping as a financial resource during armed conflict expansion.
After 2010, a compositional shift emerges: while ransom kidnapping represented the
majority of cases before 2000, its relative prevalence declined as overall figures fell,
with the proportion of non-extortionary kidnappings increasing toward a more diversified
distribution.

The use of a VEC model is methodologically appropriate for analyzing the joint
dynamics of these variables, given their non-stationarity in levels under specifications
including a constant. Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) and KPSS tests confirm that the
series exhibit non-stationary behavior consistent with an integrated process; analysis
of first differences confirms stationarity, establishing that the variables are
integrated of order one, $I(1)$. The Johansen cointegration test does not reject the
null of at most one statistically significant cointegrating vector, providing robust
evidence of a long-run equilibrium relationship. The VEC model thus allows simultaneous
modeling of short-run adjustments and long-run error correction mechanisms.

For the VEC estimation, the analysis considers ransom kidnapping ($K^{E}$),
non-extortionary kidnapping ($K^{NE}$), and the commercial bank lending rate ($r$).
Data are monthly, covering July 1954 through December 2025 ($T=858$ observations).
The optimal specification corresponds to a VEC model with 36 lags, exhibiting the
best performance in terms of AIC, absence of residual autocorrelation and
heteroskedasticity, lower RMSE, and no evidence of ARCH/GARCH effects. The
cointegrating vector $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is reported in Equation~\ref{eq_1}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq_1}
    \underset{(p < 0.001)}{0.037}\,K^{E}_t
    - \underset{(p < 0.001)}{0.072}\,K^{NE}_t
    - \underset{(p = 0.022)}{0.049}\,r_t = \tilde{\varepsilon}_t,
    \quad \tilde{\varepsilon}_t \sim I(0)
\end{equation}

Normalizing on $K^{E}_t$, the structural long-run equation is:

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.50\linewidth}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq_2}
  K^{E}_t = 1.93\,K^{NE}_t + 1.31\,r_t + \varepsilon_t
\end{equation}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

The normalized cointegrating equation identifies a long-run equilibrium in which
non-extortionary kidnapping is a key determinant of ransom kidnapping. The positive
and statistically significant coefficient on $K^{NE}$ indicates that, in the long run,
a one-unit increase in this modality is associated with an approximately 1.93-unit
increase in ransom kidnapping, \textit{ceteris paribus}. Non-extortionary kidnapping
reflects the accumulation of coercive and organizational capacity---persistent
availability of organizational resources enabling territorial control, intimidation,
and deprivation of liberty---reducing the marginal costs of shifting to the more
profitable ransom modality. The two modalities should be understood not as substitutes
but as complementary activities within a common criminal portfolio. The positive and
statistically significant relationship between the lending rate and ransom kidnapping
(coefficient 1.31) points to a mechanism consistent with liquidity constraints facing
illegal organizations: periods of monetary tightening, characterized by higher interest
rates and more limited access to financing, are associated with greater difficulties
in financing operations through legal channels, making ransom kidnapping a means of
generating immediate liquidity.

\begin{align}
  \Delta K^{E}_t &= \underset{(p < 0.01)}{-2.94}\,\tilde{\varepsilon}_{t-1}
    + \sum_{j=1}^{35}\Gamma_{1,j}\Delta\mathbf{X}_{t-j} + \varepsilon_{1,t}
  \label{eq_3}\\
  \Delta K^{NE}_t &= \underset{(p = 0.17)}{0.36}\,\tilde{\varepsilon}_{t-1}
    + \sum_{j=1}^{35}\Gamma_{2,j}\Delta\mathbf{X}_{t-j} + \varepsilon_{2,t}
  \label{eq_4}\\
  \Delta r_t &= \underset{(p = 0.10)}{-0.06}\,\tilde{\varepsilon}_{t-1}
    + \sum_{j=1}^{35}\Gamma_{3,j}\Delta\mathbf{X}_{t-j} + \varepsilon_{3,t}
  \label{eq_5}
\end{align}

where $\Gamma_{i,j\,(1\times3)}$ denotes row-parameter vectors capturing short-run
dynamics, $\Delta\mathbf{X}_{t-j\,(3\times1)}=[\Delta K^E_{t-j},\Delta K^{NE}_{t-j},
\Delta r_{t-j}]'$, and $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ are reduced-form white-noise residuals.

The adjustment coefficient for ransom kidnapping is $\alpha_{K^E}=-2.94$ (significant
at 1\%), indicating that ransom kidnapping is the primary variable through which
disequilibria are absorbed. The effective adjustment speed is
$\alpha_{K^E}\cdot\beta_{K^E}\approx-0.11$, implying that approximately 11.0\% of
any deviation from the long-run equilibrium path is corrected each period through
adjustments in ransom kidnapping---consistent with a gradual convergence process.

From the structural perspective, ransom kidnapping emerges not merely as an expression
of violence but as an adaptive economic response within a broader criminal portfolio,
linking fluctuations in conflict dynamics and macroeconomic conditions to sustained
patterns of coercive rent extraction. Its persistence reflects dynamic incentives
connecting criminal violence with organizational capacity and the macroeconomic
environment \parencite{Becker1968,Ehrlich1973,LevittMiles2006}.

To characterize the long-run stochastic structure, the permanent-transitory
decomposition of \textcite{GonzaloGranger1995} is applied. The common stochastic
trends $(f_t)$ governing the long-run behavior are expressed as linear combinations
of the observed variables capturing permanent sources of variation:

\begin{align}
  f_{1,t} &= 0.12\,K^{E}_t + 1.00\,K^{NE}_t
  \label{eq_6}\\
  f_{2,t} &= -0.02\,K^{E}_t + 1.00\,r_t
  \label{eq_7}
\end{align}

The first common factor assigns unit weight to non-extortionary kidnapping and a small
weight to ransom kidnapping, implying that long-run movements captured by this factor
are dominated by the evolution of the non-extortionary modality---reflecting sustained
criminal capacity as the persistent availability of organizational resources for
territorial control and coercion. The second common factor primarily reflects permanent
variation associated with macrofinancial conditions, responding nearly one-for-one to
changes in the lending rate (Figure~\ref{figure_7}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering

The Gonzalo-Granger decomposition separates the observed series into permanent and
transitory components.\footnote{The decomposition is based on the orthogonal
complements of the cointegrating vector $\beta$ and adjustment vector $\alpha$ in a
system with $k=3$ variables and cointegration rank $\tilde{r}=1$. The loading matrix
is $A_{GG}=\beta_{\perp}(\alpha_{\perp}'\beta_{\perp})^{-1}$. The estimated adjustment
vector $\alpha=(-2.9369,\,0.3579,\,-0.0645)'$ indicates that ransom kidnapping is the
main adjustment variable. The transitory component $\mu_t\sim I(0)$ captures
deviations from common trends.} The loading matrix representation is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq_8}
\begin{pmatrix}K^{E}_t\\K^{NE}_t\\r_t\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}1.60&1.09\\0.81&-0.13\\0.04&1.02\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}f_{1t}\\f_{2t}\end{pmatrix}
+
\begin{pmatrix}\mu_{1,t}\\\mu_{2,t}\\\mu_{3,t}\end{pmatrix},
\qquad\mu_t\sim I(0).
\end{equation}

Ransom kidnapping is exposed significantly to both common trends, with loading
coefficients of 1.60 on $f_{1t}$ and 1.09 on $f_{2t}$. Exceeding unity, these
coefficients indicate that the evolution of ransom kidnapping amplifies the persistent
shocks arising from both the accumulation of coercive capacity and long-run macroeconomic
conditions (Figure~\ref{figure_7}b). In the long run, approximately 40\% of ransom
kidnapping variability is explained by permanent shocks originating in non-extortionary
kidnapping, while the contribution of shocks associated with the interest rate decreases
progressively (Figure~\ref{figure_8}).

As a complement, the estimated Local Linear Trend (LLT) model offers a structurally
and empirically coherent representation of the dynamics of ransom kidnapping by
decomposing the observed series into a stochastic level trend, a persistent stochastic
slope, and a transitory cyclical component, as formalized in Equation~\ref{eq_9}.
Under this specification, ransom kidnapping is modeled as an $I(1)$ process, allowing
the permanent movements of the series to be distinguished from temporary cyclical
fluctuations. The estimation results indicate that all unobserved components are
statistically relevant, as reflected in the significance of the variances associated
with their innovations, with standard errors reported in parentheses.\footnote{The
significance of the parameter is assessed using Wald tests based on the asymptotic
normality of maximum-likelihood estimators, with inference conducted through
Student-\(t\) statistics, as is standard in state-space models
\parencite{Wald1943,Greene2012}.}

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Observed series:} \quad & y_t = \tau_t + c_t + \xi_t, 
&& \xi_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_\xi^2), 
&& \hat{\sigma}_\xi \approx 11.96 \; \underset{[p<0.001]}{(0.024)} \\[10pt]
\text{Stochastic trend:} \quad & \tau_t = \tau_{t-1} + b_{t-1} + \eta_t, 
&& \eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\eta^2), 
&& \hat{\sigma}_\eta \approx 7.13 \; \underset{[p<0.001]}{(0.058)} \\[10pt]
\text{Stochastic slope:} \quad & b_t = \sum_{j=1}^{8} \phi_j b_{t-j} + \zeta_t, 
&& \zeta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\zeta^2), 
&& \hat{\sigma}_\zeta \approx 3.51 \; \underset{[p<0.001]}{(0.203)} \\[10pt]
\text{Stochastic cycle:} \quad & c_t = \sum_{k=1}^{5} \rho_k c_{t-k} + \kappa_t, 
&& \kappa_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\kappa^2), 
&& \hat{\sigma}_\kappa \approx 6.77 \; \underset{[p<0.001]}{(0.083)}
\end{aligned}
\label{eq_9}
\end{equation}

In particular, the variance of the innovation in the stochastic level is estimated at
$\hat{\sigma}_{\eta}\approx 7.12$ with $p<0.001$, confirming that permanent shocks
exert a substantial influence on the long-run level of ransom kidnapping. Likewise,
the variance of the slope innovation, $\hat{\sigma}_{\zeta}\approx 3.51$ with
$p<0.001$, together with an autoregressive structure of order eight, provides strong
evidence of persistent changes in the growth rate of the series, consistent with
gradual adjustments in underlying criminal capacity rather than abrupt regime changes.
The cyclical component is also statistically significant, with estimated variance
$\hat{\sigma}_{\kappa}\approx 6.77$ and $p<0.001$, capturing medium-run fluctuations
around the long-run trend without distorting its path, while the irregular component
exhibits considerable variance, $\hat{\sigma}_{\xi}\approx 11.96$ with $p<0.001$,
indicating the presence of short-run noise and idiosyncratic shocks. Taken together,
these results show that ransom kidnapping is characterized by a dominant stochastic
trend with persistent slope dynamics, complemented by mean-reverting cyclical
movements, a structure fully consistent with the evidence obtained from the VEC model
and the Gonzalo-Granger decomposition.

Joint examination of the estimated trends, namely the one obtained from the
Gonzalo-Granger decomposition based on the VEC model and the one derived from the
Local Linear Trend (LLT) model, reveals sustained coherence over the sample period
despite being constructed from conceptually distinct econometric frameworks. From an
empirical perspective, both trends consistently reproduce the main long-run movements
in ransom kidnapping, including the sustained increase before the late 1990s, the peak
associated with the intensification of the armed conflict, and the subsequent
stabilization phase. The differences between these two trend estimates generate a
stationary residual, $I(0)$. This result indicates that the discrepancy between the two
approaches is transitory and mean-zero in nature, implying the absence of systematic
long-run divergence. From a theoretical standpoint, this convergence is consistent with
the corollary on the equivalence of stochastic trends (Corollary~\ref{cor:trend_equivalence}),
according to which different valid decompositions of an integrated series identify the
same underlying permanent component, regardless of the filtering method used.

The trend difference $d_t=\hat{\tau}_t^{VEC}-\hat{\mu}_t^{LLT}$ between the
Gonzalo-Granger permanent component and the LLT trend is stationary: the ADF test
rejects the unit root ($p=0.001$) and the KPSS test fails to reject stationarity at
12 lags ($p=0.071$). The following corollary formalizes this equivalence.

\begin{corollary}[Equivalence of stochastic trends]
\label{cor:trend-equivalence}
\label{cor:trend_equivalence}
The permanent component $\hat{\tau}_t^{VEC}$ extracted from the VEC-Gonzalo-Granger
decomposition and the LLT trend $\hat{\mu}_t^{LLT}$ track the same underlying
stochastic process: $d_t=\hat{\tau}_t^{VEC}-\hat{\mu}_t^{LLT}\sim I(0)$.
Consequently, both representations provide an equivalent macroeconomic backbone for
the structural prior calibration of the mechanism.
\end{corollary}

The cross-validation of the long-run trend in ransom kidnapping rests on the structural equivalence between the univariate stochastic component estimated through the LLT model and the common trend derived from the VEC framework. In particular, the projection of the common factors indicates that the permanent path of ransom kidnapping can be expressed as a linear combination of two latent forces shared with the rest of the system, $\tau^{GG}_t = 1.60\,f_{1,t} + 1.09\,f_{2,t}$. This representation implies that its long-run evolution is not idiosyncratic, but rather reflects exposure to structural shocks associated with the accumulation of coercive capacity and persistent macrofinancial conditions. This structure accounts for the magnitude of the level shifts captured by the LLT model and suggests that the univariate trend does not follow an isolated random walk, but aggregates the influence of the common determinants identified in the multivariate system. The stability of the difference between both trends, confirmed by the ADF and KPSS unit-root tests, provides empirical evidence consistent with the \textit{Stochastic Trend Equivalence Corollary} and reinforces the validity of the underlying cointegrating relationship. In this sense, the convergence between the trend estimated agnostically by the LLT model and the trend constructed from interactions with non-ransom kidnapping and the real interest rate supports the interpretation that these variables operate as true structural determinants of the long-run dynamics of ransom kidnapping, rather than as spurious or purely transitory relationships.

\subsection{Data Management and Sample Construction}

The data used in this section come from anonymized individual-level microdata on ransom kidnappings reported by the National Center for Historical Memory \textcite{cnmh_datos}. This institution documents kidnapping through the Information System on Events of Violence in the Colombian Armed Conflict (SIEVCAC), which integrates and validates multiple institutional sources, such as the Attorney General's Office and the National Police, as well as social sources, including NGOs, human-rights archives, and national and local press. Rather than generating primary data, the methodology compiles, harmonizes, and systematically reviews records on a case-by-case basis in order to eliminate duplicates, individualize victims, and structure each event by perpetrator, victim, date, location, and modality. The records are subsequently coded, subjected to rigorous quality-control procedures, and statistically validated before publication, producing a unified historical repository that prioritizes documentary rigor and the reconstruction of conflict-related patterns.

Starting from an initial universe of \(N=39{,}369\) observations, the data-cleaning process summarized in Table~\ref{Table_1} produced a final analytical sample of \(n=1{,}125\) cases, equivalent to 2.9\% of the total. The sample is restricted to events with confirmed ransom-related typology, valid outcome information, complete calendar records, and the key explanatory variables required for the joint estimation of the Multinomial Logit (MNL) model and the duration models. Although these restrictions imply a cumulative reduction of 97.1\% of the original universe, reflecting both the structural limitations inherent in data collection in armed-conflict settings and the high prevalence of unreported or undocumented outcomes in peripheral regions, the statistical validation exercises reported in Table~\ref{Table_2} indicate that this reduction does not compromise econometric consistency or the internal validity of the results.

\subsection{Strategic Determinants and Temporal Dynamics of Kidnapping}

Based on a final sample of 1,125 observations, the MNL model is estimated to analyze the determinants of kidnapping outcomes. This specification is particularly suitable for the multicategorical and unordered nature of the dependent variable, which captures mutually exclusive outcomes such as Payment, Death, Rescue, and Escape or Release. Unlike binary or linear regression models, the MNL framework is explicitly designed for risk environments in which perpetrators and the State interact strategically. Its relevance in this context lies in its ability to quantify how a vector of explanatory variables changes the probability that a negotiation departs from the baseline outcome of Payment toward failed or violent resolutions. By decomposing the net effects of organizational, territorial, and institutional determinants, the model provides a granular view of the incentive structure governing kidnapping behavior, allowing an empirical assessment of whether ransom kidnapping operates according to a rational market logic or is shaped by criminal frictions.

The estimated econometric model is presented below. The estimation of the parameters specified in Equation~\ref{MNL} is carried out by relaxing the assumption that the error terms are independent and identically distributed, in light of the spatial nature of the armed conflict. Under this premise, kidnapping events occurring within the same jurisdiction are assumed to share unobserved institutional and operational determinants, such as local law-enforcement capacity, topographic conditions, or the historical presence of armed groups, which may induce correlation in the error terms. Consequently, the variance matrix is adjusted using robust standard errors clustered at the municipal level, covering a total of 373 territorial units, in order to correct for intracluster correlation and ensure statistically robust inference.\footnote{Clustering standard errors at the municipal level accounts for the fact that kidnapping acts within the same municipality may share unobserved characteristics, such as local institutional capacity, geographic constraints, or the presence of armed groups. These factors may induce correlation in the error terms across observations within the same group, violating the assumption of independent errors. For example, a temporary weakening of law-enforcement capacity in a municipality may increase the probability of ransom payments in multiple cases. Ignoring this intramunicipal correlation may lead to underestimated standard errors and overstated statistical significance. Clustering addresses this dependence and supports consistent inference.}

To analyze the determinants of kidnapping outcomes, an MNL model is estimated, where $Y_{i,m}$ denotes the outcome of kidnapping $i$ in municipality $m$. The dependent variable is numerically coded and takes one of four mutually exclusive outcomes indexed by $j \in \{0,1,2,3\}$. Specifically, $j=0$ denotes escape or release without payment, $j=1$ corresponds to ransom payment, $j=2$ indicates the death of the victim, and $j=3$ represents GAULA rescue. This categorical structure ensures that each kidnapping event is uniquely classified into a single outcome. Taking payment, $j=1$, as the base category, which represents successful rent extraction by the criminal, the model estimates the probabilities of observing each alternative outcome \(j \in \{0,2,3\}\) relative to payment as a function of the relevant covariates and municipal characteristics.

\begin{equation} \label{MNL}
\ln \left( \frac{\Pr(Y_{i,m} = j \mid \mathbf{X}_{i,m})}{\Pr(Y_{i,m} = 1 \mid \mathbf{X}_{i,m})} \right)
=
\alpha_j
+
\underbrace{\mathbf{G}_i' \boldsymbol{\beta}_{1,j}}_{\text{Perpetrator}}
+
\underbrace{\mathbf{V}_i' \boldsymbol{\beta}_{2,j}}_{\text{Victim}}
+
\underbrace{\mathbf{C}_{i,m}' \boldsymbol{\beta}_{3,j}}_{\text{Context}}
+
\underbrace{\mathbf{T}_i' \boldsymbol{\beta}_{4,j}}_{\text{Tactics}},
\qquad j \in \{0,2,3\}.
\end{equation}

\noindent Where $\alpha_j$ denotes the alternative-specific intercept for outcome $j$, and $j=1$ is the base category. The full vector of covariates is defined as the concatenation $\mathbf{X}_{i,m} = [\mathbf{G}_i', \mathbf{V}_i', \mathbf{C}_{i,m}', \mathbf{T}_i']' \in \mathbb{R}^{26}$. The model is estimated by maximum likelihood under the standard MNL assumption that the unobserved components of utility are independently and identically distributed with a Type-I extreme-value distribution. Standard errors are clustered at the municipal level to account for possible intramunicipal correlation in unobserved determinants.

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{G}_i \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (\textit{Perpetrator}): A column vector that identifies the armed organization. It includes specific variables for the ELN, paramilitary groups, and common crime. The estimates are relative to the FARC baseline.
    \item $\mathbf{V}_i \in \mathbb{R}^6$ (\textit{Victim}): Captures victim characteristics. The vector comprises four dummy variables for socioeconomic profiles, including middle class, vulnerable population, public-sector employees, and unknown profiles relative to the high-income baseline. It also includes indicators for female victims and missing gender information relative to the male baseline.
    \item $\mathbf{C}_{i,m} \in \mathbb{R}^8$ (\textit{Context}): Controls for the geostrategic environment. It includes four regional indicators for Andean, Caribbean, Pacific, and Eastern territories relative to metropolitan areas. In addition, it incorporates dummies for the 1998--2002, 2003--2010, and post-2011 periods relative to the pre-1998 era, together with the continuous measure of the log of total victims.
    \item $\mathbf{T}_i \in \mathbb{R}^9$ (\textit{Tactics}): Accounts for operational heterogeneity. This vector contains four indicator variables for capture methods, including roadblocks, armed incursions, deception tactics, and unknown methods relative to direct attacks. It also includes an indicator for collective kidnapping structure, three indicator variables for short, long, and missing duration categories relative to express captivity, and a variable for GAULA intervention.
\end{itemize}

The coefficient vectors $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{k,j}$ associated with each structural component $k \in \{1, \dots, 4\}$ are estimated by maximum likelihood. To ensure valid inference, standard errors are clustered at the municipal level ($m$), accounting for unobserved spatial correlation in institutional capacity and local conflict dynamics.

The main estimation results are reported in terms of Relative Risk Ratios (RRR) in Table~\ref{tab:mlogit_results}, while the full set of estimated coefficients is presented in Appendix~B. In the MNL framework, estimated coefficients represent changes in the log odds of observing outcome \(j\) relative to the base category, while the RRR corresponds to exponentiated coefficients and measures multiplicative changes in relative probabilities, offering a more intuitive interpretation. Standard errors are clustered at the municipal level, accounting for 373 distinct groups, to address possible intramunicipal correlation and heteroskedasticity. The model is jointly statistically significant, as indicated by a Wald statistic of \(\chi^2(78)=5{,}250.34\) with \(p<0.0001\), rejecting the null hypothesis that the explanatory variables are jointly irrelevant. The Pseudo \(R^2\) of 0.1847 indicates a reasonable goodness of fit for a discrete-choice model in a conflict setting.

For a correct interpretation of the findings, it is crucial to understand that the coefficients are presented as RRR. These do not indicate absolute probabilities, but rather quantify how many times more likely a specific outcome is to occur relative to the normative base category of the model: Payment (\(Y=1\)). Likewise, categorical variables are interpreted relative to a reference group. For example, when analyzing the ``Perpetrator'' group, the ELN or common-crime coefficients show how relative risk changes compared with the behavior of the reference group, the FARC. An RRR greater than 1 indicates that the event is more likely than payment compared with the FARC, whereas an RRR below 1 suggests a stronger tendency toward effective ransom extraction.

In this context, during the contemporary period (Peace/Current), the relative probability of a ``failed kidnapping'' (Escape or Release without payment) increases by a factor of 11.28 relative to the payment base, compared with the pre-1998 era. This effect is statistically significant (\(p<0.01\)) and provides empirical support for the deterrence framework proposed by \textcite{Mejia2000Secuestro}, suggesting that the consolidation of specialized GAULA units has substantially weakened the financial incentives underlying the crime. In practical terms, this implies that under current institutional conditions, kidnapping is much more likely to end in forced release or abandonment, constituting a clear financial loss for the perpetrator, than in successful ransom extraction. Consequently, the evidence reflects a structural shift from income-generating criminal activity toward a high-risk environment characterized by a markedly greater probability of failure.

It is worth emphasizing that market equilibrium is altered by adverse-selection mechanisms inherent in certain victim profiles. Target-population heterogeneity introduces non-trivial distortions; in particular, the selection of public-sector officials systematically degrades the probability of success, altering the criminal's risk calculation. The model indicates that for this group, the relative probability of ``Escape or Release'' is 3.92 times higher than that of a successful payment (\(p<0.01\)) compared with upper-class victims. This finding corroborates that political or state-related kidnapping operates under incentives distinct from purely economic ones: the State, by maintaining a strict policy of non-monetary negotiation and imposing a high political and media cost on the captor, makes these hostages financially unviable. This pushes outcomes toward release or escape due to institutional pressure, rather than toward the monetary transaction typical of extortive kidnapping.

However, despite these improvements in state capacity and the distortion of the market, the model identifies a critical limit in rescue technology governed by information asymmetry. The relative probability of rescue decreases monotonically as captivity duration extends, with the RRR falling from 0.18 in the short run, less than one month, to 0.08 in the long run (\(p<0.001\)). This decay indicates that state efficiency is strictly time-dependent; a victim is more than twice as likely to be successfully rescued during the first weeks of the event as in later months. This evidence corroborates the hypothesis that rescue operations depend on ``hot intelligence'' during the initial urban or transit phases. By contrast, once the victim is absorbed into the geographic ``rear guard'' controlled by the perpetrator, information asymmetry becomes insurmountable, making military exfiltration increasingly unlikely and shifting the bargaining advantage to the captors.

Within this framework, public policy must prioritize neutralizing factors that increase lethality and criminal resilience, focusing specifically on the perpetrator's logistical bottlenecks. The analysis of lethality determinants highlights a critical inverse relationship between organizational complexity and violence: common crime exhibits an RRR for death of 63.63 (\(p<0.01\)) relative to the FARC baseline. This finding supports the hypothesis that lethality is a function of logistical constraints. The lack of infrastructure for long-term retention pushes these actors into a compressed time horizon where the absence of immediate payment precipitates the elimination of the victim to mitigate detection risks. For mechanism design, the State must increase the cost of immediate retention, forcing the criminal to release the victim early before the execution incentive is triggered.

The spatial analysis reveals a consistent center-periphery pattern. Using the Metropolis as the reference category, peripheral regions such as the Andean and Caribbean regions present RRR values consistently below one. This indicates that failed kidnapping outcomes are considerably less likely in peripheral regions than in major urban centers. The Caribbean region provides a clear example of the state-capacity hypothesis. While deterrence and GAULA response infrastructure are effective in the metropolis, forcing operational failures, the periphery offers an environment of lower institutional friction favorable to rent extraction. In addition, the findings indicate that the time factor undermines the State's response capacity. While express kidnapping entails high exposure to immediate operational friction, once captivity exceeds one month the criminal successfully consolidates control over the victim, reflected in a coefficient of 0.079. In this context, prolonged duration acts as a stabilization mechanism that minimizes operational risk and substantially reduces the probability of successful rescue.

Finally, regarding institutional response, GAULA intervention exhibits an RRR of 1.39 for the rescue outcome, representing a strategic factor that, while showing a \(p\)-value of 0.34, underscores a critical area for policy improvement. Although this coefficient does not reach conventional levels of statistical significance, its magnitude suggests positive pressure on tactical outcomes by increasing the risk of capture during the initial stages of kidnapping. This lack of statistical significance likely reflects the high variability of operational success and the ``cold intelligence'' barrier inherent in later stages of captivity. Consequently, strengthening GAULA intervention mechanisms is essential to transform this latent tactical pressure into a robust deterrent effect, effectively shifting the risk-benefit ratio against the perpetrator from the very beginning of the criminal act.

The analysis of kidnapping duration requires the explicit incorporation of the temporal dimension, recognizing that the strategic dynamics of negotiation are evolutionary rather than static processes. Within this framework, survival curves constitute an appropriate methodological tool for addressing temporality, since they describe the probability of remaining in a given state over time, as formally articulated by \textcite{Bradburn2003}. In the context of ransom kidnapping, these functions estimate the conditional probability that a victim remains in captivity at each point in time without ransom payment having occurred. Tracing this trajectory makes it possible to identify structural heterogeneity in captivity persistence across different armed actors, thereby providing a dynamic perspective on outcomes that extends beyond the limitations of traditional static models.

Formally, the analysis is based on the proportional hazards model proposed by \textcite{Cox1972}. This framework models the hazard rate \(h(t \mid \mathbf{X})\), defined as the instantaneous probability that the event occurs at time \(t\) conditional on survival up to that moment. The rate is expressed as the product of two specific components: an unspecified nonparametric baseline hazard \(h_0(t)\), which captures the underlying duration dependence common to all subjects, and a parametric exponential function. As shown in Equation~\ref{equation_11}, the latter component incorporates the vector of explanatory variables \(\mathbf{X}\) and its associated coefficients \(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\):

\begin{equation}
    h(t \mid \mathbf{X}) = h_0(t)\exp\left(\mathbf{X}'\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\right)
    \label{equation_11}
\end{equation}

Integrating the hazard function over the interval \([0,t]\) yields the cumulative hazard function \(H(t \mid \mathbf{X})\), which represents the total accumulated risk up to time \(t\). Under the proportional-hazards assumption, Equation~\ref{equation_12} shows that this function can be expressed as the product of the baseline cumulative hazard \(H_0(t)\) and the individual risk score \(\exp(\mathbf{X}'\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}})\):

\begin{equation}
    H(t \mid \mathbf{X}) = \int_0^t h(u \mid \mathbf{X})\, \mathrm{d}u
    = \exp\left(\mathbf{X}'\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\right) H_0(t),
    \quad \text{where }
    H_0(t) = \int_0^t h_0(u)\, \mathrm{d}u
    \label{equation_12}
\end{equation}

Finally, the survival function \(S(t \mid \mathbf{X})\) denotes the probability that the event has not occurred by time \(t\). As derived in Equation~\ref{equation_13}, this function is expressed as the exponential of the negative cumulative hazard, implying that the specific values of the explanatory variables proportionally scale the baseline survival probability:

\begin{equation}
    S(t \mid \mathbf{X}) = \exp\left( -H(t \mid \mathbf{X}) \right) = \exp\left( - \exp\left(\mathbf{X}'\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\right) H_0(t) \right)
    \label{equation_13}
\end{equation}

The coefficients \(\tilde{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) are estimated using maximum partial likelihood, following \textcite{Cox1972}. This approach allows consistent estimation of covariate effects without requiring specification of the functional form of the baseline hazard \(h_0(t)\), thereby making the model semiparametric. Since duration is measured in days, tied event times may arise, meaning that multiple payments can be observed at exactly the same duration; in such cases, the approximation proposed by \textcite{Bradburn2003} is used to adjust the partial likelihood without altering the structure of the model. In addition, standard errors are computed using the robust sandwich estimator, which accounts for potential heteroskedasticity and ensures valid statistical inference without imposing additional distributional assumptions.

In this sense, it can be observed that during the first 20 days, the probability of remaining in captivity exceeds 0.85 for both the FARC and the ELN, while for common criminal groups it declines more rapidly toward values close to 0.70, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{figure_12b}. Around day 60, the probability of remaining in captivity is approximately 0.56 for the FARC, 0.50 for the ELN, around 0.37 for paramilitary groups, and only 0.10 for common criminals. By day 180, these differences persist, with probabilities close to 0.29 for the FARC, 0.23 for the ELN, 0.12 for paramilitaries, and practically zero for common criminal groups. These patterns indicate that insurgent organizations tend to maintain captivity for longer periods before payment occurs, while decentralized criminal groups resolve events more quickly. From a strategic perspective, the evidence suggests that kidnapping duration and the timing of ransom payment are closely linked to organizational capacity, territorial control, and operational structure, reflecting systematic differences in captivity management and negotiation dynamics across armed actors.

The temporal dimension of kidnapping events constitutes a fundamental component for understanding the strategic interaction between perpetrators and the State, since the analysis should not be limited to determining whether a ransom payment occurs, but must also account for the speed at which it materializes. While MNL models estimate the relative probabilities of alternative outcomes, the survival curves proposed by \textcite{Bradburn2003} provide a dynamic representation of the probability that captivity persists beyond a given duration, thereby characterizing the temporal evolution of the event process. By contrast, the proportional hazards model developed by \textcite{Cox1972} identifies differences in the instantaneous rate at which ransom payments occur over time, as formally expressed in Equation~\ref{equation_11}. Consequently, this latter approach is essential for capturing the strategic timing of negotiation outcomes, allowing a more precise assessment of how explanatory variables influence not only the probability that payment occurs but also the speed at which negotiations are resolved.

Within this framework, the hazard coefficients derived from the proportional hazards model developed by \textcite{Cox1972} allow the identification of systematic differences in the instantaneous rate of ransom payment across armed actors. Taking the FARC as the reference category, the results reveal substantial heterogeneity in the retention technology employed by different groups. Kidnappings perpetrated by paramilitary groups show a hazard ratio of 4.28 (\(p<0.001\)), indicating that, holding all other variables constant, the instantaneous rate of ransom payment is more than four times higher than that observed for the FARC. In practical terms, if on a given day both groups still hold the victim captive, the immediate probability that ransom is paid at that moment is substantially higher in kidnappings carried out by paramilitary groups than in those perpetrated by the FARC. This pattern indicates that paramilitary organizations display greater urgency to conclude the event through ransom collection, reflecting shorter negotiation horizons and a faster monetization strategy.

Similarly, common criminal groups present a hazard ratio of 1.71 (\(p=0.021\)), indicating a faster resolution of the event. By contrast, the ELN does not show statistically significant differences relative to the FARC, suggesting symmetry in insurgent operational practices. From a strategic perspective, these findings reveal a structural dichotomy in organizational behavior. Paramilitary and common criminal groups tend to operate under a high-turnover logic characterized by shorter time horizons, while consolidated insurgent organizations such as the FARC and the ELN maintain longer negotiations, consistent with greater territorial control and a more developed bureaucratic capacity to withstand the operational pressure exerted by the State.

A fundamental finding for public-policy design is the drastic reduction in the payment hazard rate observed when the victim belongs to the public sector. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:cox_complete}, this indicator records a value of 0.199 (\(p<0.001\)) relative to the upper class, which serves as the reference category. In this context, the hazard rate represents the instantaneous probability that a payment event occurs at a given moment, conditional on the captivity situation having persisted up to that moment. This ratio indicates that the speed of completion of a payment for a public official decreases by 80.1\% relative to the comparison baseline.

This economic disincentive is reinforced by an asymmetry in the State's tactical response capacity. When rescue events are examined, the results reported in the second column of Table~\ref{tab:cox_complete} show that public-official cases present a hazard ratio of 0.237 (\(p=0.001\)). Although this value indicates that rescues in this segment tend to unfold over a longer time horizon relative to civilians, its joint interpretation with the ransom-payment hazard reveals a form of operational paralysis for the captor. The criminal organization faces a strategic dilemma in which the probability of securing an economic transaction is very low, while the risk of eventual state intervention persists throughout a captivity that becomes increasingly costly and difficult to sustain.

Finally, the analysis of the contemporary period identifies an efficiency scissors that maximizes the deterrent effect by contrasting the current era with the pre-1998 period. In the Peace Process/Current period, beginning in 2011, resolution through payment has become 49\% slower, registering a hazard ratio of 0.510 (\(p=0.035\)). Simultaneously, the speed of successful rescues has increased drastically, reaching a hazard ratio of 4.863 (\(p<0.001\)). As shown in Table~\ref{tab:cox_complete}, this dynamic generates a structural collapse in the profitability of kidnapping; the perpetrator faces a scenario in which the time required to obtain a benefit is significantly longer, while the window of vulnerability to state intervention has closed exponentially, drastically reducing the strategic viability of this activity.

\subsection{Population-Level Kidnapping Risk: Evidence from a National Survey}
\label{subsec:encuesta-nacional}

This section draws on the Survey on Coexistence and Citizen Security (ECSC) \textcite{DANE_ECSC_2021}, whose official methodology and operational guidelines are documented in the Fieldwork Manual and Basic Concepts \textcite{dane2022manual} and in the General Methodological Framework of the ECSC \textcite{dane2022ecsc}. The ECSC is a nationally representative probabilistic survey of the population aged 15 and over, with national, urban, and rural coverage. It is designed to measure direct victimization, perceptions of security, and reporting behavior, including extortion and, to a lesser extent, kidnapping for ransom. Compared with \textcite{cnmh_datos}, its main strength lies in its sampling design and in the use of expansion factors that allow population-level inference, as well as in its capacity to capture unreported incidents and provide standardized, territorially comparable evidence on victimization dynamics.

On this basis, the section develops a statistical profile of ransom-kidnapping victims in Colombia and identifies individual characteristics that are not observable in \textcite{cnmh_datos}. A logistic regression model is then estimated to examine whether these idiosyncratic attributes are associated with patterns of victim selection by criminal networks, although their statistical relevance remains limited. The dependent variable is binary and indicates whether a surveyed person has been a kidnapping victim. The explanatory variables include educational attainment, gender, socioeconomic stratum, age-group fixed effects, and year fixed effects for the 2018--2021 period.

In addition, the ECSC is not designed to document ransom kidnapping in detailed case-by-case fashion, and the reported events show low statistical incidence within the sample. The information is self-reported and may therefore be subject to memory bias or underreporting. Moreover, because its unit of observation is the person rather than the specific criminal act, the survey does not allow reconstruction of the organizational structures, operational modalities, or long-run conflict patterns with the same depth as the case-by-case methodology employed by \textcite{cnmh_datos} and presented in the previous section. Consequently, the ECSC should be interpreted as a complementary population-based source rather than as a substitute for specialized conflict records.

The descriptive and econometric results based on the ECSC survey indicate that, after applying population expansion factors, the estimated number of ransom-kidnapping victims between 2018 and 2021 amounts to 34,255 cases, with a clear increase in 2020 and 2021. Men represent 58.0\% of victims, especially from 2020 onward, while cases are concentrated in socioeconomic strata 2 and 3, suggesting that victimization is not limited to the lowest or highest income levels. The largest shares are observed among persons with secondary and higher education. However, once the logistic model is estimated, most explanatory variables lose statistical significance. The only exception is the category ``Up to primary education'', which is associated with a significantly lower probability of victimization relative to the reference group, defined as persons with complete secondary education. Average marginal effects are extremely small in absolute terms, reflecting the low prevalence of the event in the general population. Nevertheless, the model shows moderate discriminatory capacity, with an area under the ROC curve (AUC) of 0.72, indicating that when a victim and a non-victim are randomly compared, the model assigns a higher predicted probability to the actual victim in approximately 72\% of cases. Overall, these findings suggest that ransom kidnapping is a low-prevalence phenomenon that is only weakly explained by idiosyncratic sociodemographic characteristics, although it is not entirely random, consistent with its strategic and selective nature in the Colombian context.

\section{Relevance of the Empirical Framework for the Mechanism Model}
\label{sec:pertinencia}

The empirical evidence does not constitute a block separate from the theoretical model,
but rather the source of its restrictions and parameters. The VEC system identifies
macroeconomic persistence and long-run relationships that discipline retention and
financing costs. The multinomial logit and competing-risks models provide probabilities
of payment, death, rescue, and release, as well as heterogeneity by perpetrator, victim,
region, and duration. These estimates determine the priors $\mu_0$, the intensities
$\tilde\lambda_j$, the structural footprints $\beta_{K,j}(\theta_K)$, and the social
loss margins used by the mechanism.

This connection fulfills two functions. First, it prevents type identification from
depending on arbitrarily imposed criminal categories: types are distinguished by
observable differences in captivity technologies, outcomes, and sensitivity to state
instruments. Second, it makes it possible to interpret policy as dual control:
$\alpha_t$ and $\gamma_t$ simultaneously modify the material risk of the episode and the
information available for later decisions. Thus, the empirical part anchors the prior
and the theoretical part transforms that information into an implementable dynamic rule.

\section{Theoretical Model}
\label{sec:model}
\label{sec:introduccion-mechanism}

\noindent In Colombia, ransom kidnapping follows a non-linear trajectory that reveals
\textbf{dynamic strategic interaction} under \textbf{incomplete information}. In this
environment, heterogeneity among captors, variability in the state response, and
household vulnerability configure a system of interdependent decisions. The mechanism
formalized in this section models the chaining of payments, rescues, and operational
pressure as a robust Bayesian learning process whose fundamental result is the
\textbf{Theorem on the Identification of Rational Types in Unknown Environments}. Under
this framework, state inference does not collapse in the face of surprising actions;
instead, it distinguishes between strategic intention, imperfect physical execution,
and statistical signal. To do so, the ``Mano de Dios--Guadalupe'' (MDG) process
incorporates a perturbation akin to \textbf{trembling-hand} perfection in
\textcite{Selten1975Perfectness}, refined through the notion of \textbf{sequential
equilibrium} in \textcite{KrepsWilson1982}. This integration allows initially
low-probability events to be treated as infinitesimal deviations (trembles), ensuring
that the planner maintains a consistent and rational belief system even off the
equilibrium path. Consistent with the principles of \textbf{Perfect Bayesian
Equilibrium} in \textcite{FudenbergTirole1991}, beliefs about the captor type
$\theta_K$ are disciplined by Bayes updating and uninterrupted sequential consistency.
If public policy generates asymptotically separable competing risks, the theorem shows
that the accumulation of signals audits transitory volatility and forces convergence of
the posterior distribution toward the structural identity of the true type. Thus, the
normative objective goes beyond \textbf{reducing criminal profitability} and
\textbf{minimizing social loss}, in line with \textcite{Becker1968} and
\textcite{Mejia2000Secuestro}; it seeks to \textbf{optimize the architecture of
inference} that allows the State to identify the nature of the adversary, in continuity
with the strategic approach of \textcite{Selten1977Kidnapping}.

\subsection{Environment, Types, and Deterrence Mechanism}
\label{subsec:acciones}

Let $\mathcal{J}=\{S,K,F\}$ be the set of \textbf{agents}, where $S$ denotes the State,
$K$ the kidnapper, and $F$ the family. Each episode is characterized by the following
vector of \textbf{types}:
\[
\theta=(\theta_K,\theta_F,\theta_V,\theta_S)\in\Theta
=\Theta_K\times\Theta_F\times\Theta_V\times\Theta_S.
\]
Each component of $\theta$ captures an economic dimension of the episode. Thus,
$\theta_K\in\Theta_K$ describes the \textbf{kidnapper type} and its organization or
criminal technology, with
$\Theta_K=\{\text{DC},\text{PAR},\text{ELN},\text{FARC}\}$; $\theta_F\in\Theta_F$
represents the \textbf{family type} and approximates payment capacity, with
$\Theta_F=\{\text{High wealth},\text{Low wealth}\}$; $\theta_V\in\Theta_V$ identifies
the \textbf{victim type} according to observable exposure and visibility attributes,
with $\Theta_V=\{\text{Public},\text{Private}\}$; and $\theta_S\in\Theta_S$ summarizes
the \textbf{institutional environment}, with $\Theta_S=\{\text{Hard},\text{Lax}\}$.
In addition, each episode is located in an observable geographic zone
$z\in\mathcal{R}$, with
$\mathcal{R}=\{\text{Metropolitan},\text{Andean},\text{Caribbean},\text{Pacific},
\text{East--Jungle}\}$. Location $z$ is not a private strategic type, but a public
initial condition of the episode. Its first role is to discipline the prior over the
captor type,
$\mu_0(\theta\mid z,\theta_V)=\ProbE(\theta_K=\theta\mid z,\theta_V)$; in this
mechanism only $\theta_K$ is taken to be \textbf{private} to $K$, the State and the
family do not observe it, and they maintain a belief
$\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$. The other components and the geographic location $z$ are
\textbf{observable} at the opening of the episode.

At date $t$, the \textbf{public history} $h_t\in H_t$ recursively records public
instruments, executed actions, and outcomes up to the beginning of $t$
\textup{(}\eqref{eq:historia-publica}--\eqref{eq:actualizacion-historia}\textup{)}.
By construction, $h_t$ does not include the type vector $\theta$ nor the belief
$\mu_t$; the types $(\theta_F,\theta_V,\theta_S)$ and $z$ are observable at the opening
of the episode but are not components of the tuple. The belief
$\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$ is updated by Bayes throughout the episode.

The \textbf{information sets} when choosing at $t$ are
\[
\mathcal{I}_t^S = \bigl(h_t, z, \theta_F, \theta_V, \theta_S, \mu_t\bigr),
\qquad
\mathcal{I}_t^F = \bigl(h_t, z, \theta_F, \theta_V, \theta_S\bigr),
\qquad
\mathcal{I}_t^K = \bigl(h_t, z, \theta_K, \theta_F, \theta_V, \theta_S\bigr).
\]
Although $\mu_t$ is internal to the State, its exclusion from the private information
sets creates no inconsistency: in the PBE, private agents share the same prior and the
same public history $h_t$, so they can replicate the planner's Bayesian update
\textcite{FudenbergTirole1991}. For the captor, $\mu_{t+1}$ is a strategic object that
anticipates future state policy, not a source of uncertainty about its own type.

The period protocol is decomposed into a sequence of allocations, intentions, and
stochastic materializations. At each instant $t$, agents formulate optimal strategic
intentions $a_t^{i\ast}$, derived from their respective optimization programs subject
to Individual Rationality (IR) and Incentive Compatibility (IC) constraints. Define the
\textbf{space of institutional allocations} as the product
$\mathcal{X}_t:=\mathcal{A}^{S,\mathrm{disc}}\times\mathcal{A}^{\alpha}\times
\mathcal{A}^{\gamma}$, where the State selects both the discrete action
$a_t^{S\ast}$ and the continuous instrument vector
$(\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)$. The continuous instruments take values in the unit
interval: $\alpha_t\in[0,1]$ is the \textbf{financial blocking rate} \textup{(the
fraction of the ransom blocked by the State)} and $\gamma_t\in[0,1]$ is the
\textbf{intensity of operational pressure} \textup{(normalized between zero and maximum
institutional capacity)}; both are observable public-policy instruments.

The transition between agents' strategic intentions and their effective materialization
is mediated by a stochastic implementation operator called the \textbf{MDG process}.
In each period, this operator transforms the discrete intentions
$(a_t^{F\ast},a_t^{K\ast},a_t^{S\ast})$ into executed actions
$\tilde{A}_t=(\tilde{a}_t^F,\tilde{a}_t^K,\tilde{a}_t^S)$, which enter the public
record and determine $h_{t+1}$ and the public signal $\zeta_t$. The State's continuous
instruments enter as the design optima $(\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)$, formally
defined in Section~\ref{sec:state-mechanism}; no additional continuous materialization
is introduced. Conditional on those executed actions and on the optimal institutional
policy, the system determines the physical outcome $m_t\in\mathcal{M}$ and the
detection signal $d_t\in\{0,1\}$ through a competing-risks block.

The physical outcome $m_t\in\mathcal{M}$ belongs to the continuation set
$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{cont}}=\{\mathrm{cont}\}$ or to the absorbing set
$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{term}}=\{\mathrm{pay},\mathrm{res},\mathrm{rel},\mathrm{kill}\}$.
Separately, let $s_t\in\{0,1\}$ be the \textbf{binary survival indicator of the victim}
at the close of the period's operational margin, where $s_t=1$ means that the victim
survives and $s_t=0$ means that the victim does not survive. This indicator is used in
the rescue/cooperation branch and does not redefine the categorical support of $m_t$.

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:historia-publica}
h_t = \bigl(t,\, \alpha^\ast_{[0,t-1]},\, \gamma^\ast_{[0,t-1]},\,
\tilde{a}^F_{[0,t-1]},\, \tilde{a}^K_{[0,t-1]},\, \tilde{a}^S_{[0,t-1]},\,
m_{[0,t-1]},\, d_{[0,t-1]}\bigr)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:actualizacion-historia}
h_{t+1} = \bigl(h_t,\, \alpha_t^\ast,\, \gamma_t^\ast,\, \tilde{a}_t^F,\,
\tilde{a}_t^K,\, \tilde{a}_t^S,\, m_t,\, d_t\bigr).
\end{equation}

The horizon is the endogenous stopping time\footnote{The stopping-time condition
guarantees that $\{\tau=t\}$ is measurable with respect to $h_t$.}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stopping-time}
\tau = \inf \{ t \geq 0 : m_t \in \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{term}} \}.
\end{equation}

The dynamic kidnapping-deterrence mechanism is formally defined as the tuple
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{D} =
\bigl\langle \mathcal{J},\, \mathbb{T},\, \Theta,\, \mathcal{R},\,
(\mathcal{X}_t)_{t \in \mathbb{T}},\, (H_t)_{t \in \mathbb{T}},\,
(\mu_t)_{t \in \mathbb{T}},\, \ProbE \bigr\rangle.
\end{equation*}
The social choice rule is implementable if it constitutes the path of a \textbf{PBE}
\textcite{FudenbergTirole1991}: the pair $(s,\mu)$ with $s=(s^S,s^K,s^F)$ and
$\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$. Given $z\in\mathcal{R}$ and $\theta_K\sim\mu_0$, the
protocol deploys in each $t$ the measurable public-policy rule
\[
\chi_t \colon H_t\times\mathcal{R} \longrightarrow
\mathcal{A}^{S,\text{disc}} \times \mathcal{A}^{\alpha} \times
\mathcal{A}^{\gamma}, \qquad s^S = \chi = (\chi_t)_{t\in\mathbb{T}},
\]
interacting with private responses in $\mathcal{I}_t^K$ and $\mathcal{I}_t^F$. The PBE
is the appropriate solution concept because it guarantees that the State cannot be
systematically deceived by the captor: its beliefs $\mu_t$ are coherent with the
actions actually observed, so operational pressure $\gamma_t^\ast$ is calibrated on
correct inferences about the type $\theta_K$ and not on assumptions inconsistent with
equilibrium.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Sequential rationality.} For each player $i\in\{K,F\}$, the optimal
  equilibrium \textbf{intention} $a_t^{i\ast}$ satisfies
  \[
  a_t^{i\ast}\in\arg\max_{a_t^i\in\mathcal{A}^i}
  \mathcal{U}_t^i(a_t^i\mid\mathcal{I}_t^i,\chi,s^{-i}),
  \]
  while the planner selects the triple
  $(a_t^{S\ast},\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)$ to minimize expected social loss
  weighted by $\mu_t$. The \textbf{social choice rule} is formalized through the
  measurable map $f_t:\mathcal{I}_t^S\to\mathcal{X}_t$, so that
  \[
  x_t^\ast:=f_t(\mathcal{I}_t^S)=
  (a_t^{S\ast},\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)\in\mathcal{X}_t.
  \]
  This vector summarizes the public policy in equilibrium; the MDG process then
  transforms intentions into executed actions $\tilde{A}_t$ entering the public record.

  \item \textbf{Bayesian consistency on the path.} Let $\ProbE^s$ denote the ex ante
  law over trajectories induced by strategy profile $s$, prior $\mu_0$, and captivity
  dynamics. At every node reached with positive probability under $\ProbE^s$, beliefs
  coincide with Bayesian inference. For every $\theta\in\Theta_K$ and $t\ge1$,
  \[
  \mu_t(\theta)=\ProbE^s(\theta_K=\theta\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}),
  \qquad
  \mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}=\sigma(z,\theta_V,\zeta_0,\dots,\zeta_{t-1}).
  \]
  This condition is the core of the identification mechanism: by requiring $\mu_t$ to
  be coherent with the actions observed under $s$, it guarantees that
  $\gamma_t^\ast$ is informative about $\theta_K$.
\end{enumerate}

Although the Revelation Principle guarantees equivalence with a direct mechanism, the
indirect approach is deliberately adopted: it allows observable public-policy
instruments---financial blocks and operational pressure---to be modeled as they are
deployed in practice, rather than reducing the State to a designer of counterfactual
messages.

\subsection{Probabilistic Technology, Implementation, and Likelihoods}
\label{sec:probability-technology}

This subsection groups the stochastic technology of the mechanism before presenting
the agents' optimization problems. The logical sequence is: optimal intentions
$a_t^{i\ast}$ materialize as executed actions $\tilde a_t^i$ through the MDG process;
executed actions, together with instruments $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$, induce competing
risks and public signals; finally, those signals generate likelihoods and update belief
$\mu_t$ by Bayes. Thus, when the objective functions of $K$, $F$, and $S$ are
introduced, all probabilistic objects entering their payoffs have already been defined.

\subsubsection{Stochastic Implementation: MDG Process}
\label{subsec:discrete}
\label{sec:estructura-juego}
\label{sec:mano-de-dios}

This section formalizes the stochastic architecture that transforms strategic plans
into observable realizations. The MDG implementation filter converts each agent's
optimal intention $a_t^{i\ast}$ into an executed action $\tilde a_t^i$ with full support
over $\mathcal{A}^i$; the logit distribution implementing it is axiomatically justified
as the unique law compatible with full support, dominance, and maximum entropy
\textup{(}Remark~\ref{rem:axiomas-logit}\textup{)}. The physical-outcome law
$\ProbE(\cdot\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)$ determines the observable outcome $m_t$
conditional on the material state of the period, including the executed triple.
Finally, the inverse-transform mechanism operationalizes both layers in a reproducible
sequence of draws.

The MDG process transforms intentions $a_t^{i\ast}$ into executed actions
$\tilde a_t^i$ through an implementation law concentrated on $a_t^{i\ast}$ but with
full support over $\mathcal{A}^i$, in the spirit of the \textit{trembling hand} of
\textcite{Selten1975Perfectness} and \textcite{KrepsWilson1982}. The executed action of
each agent $i\in\{K,S,F\}$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mano-dios-jugador}
\tilde{a}_t^i \sim \ProbI{i}\bigl(\cdot \mid a_t^{i\ast},\, X_t\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $X_t$ summarizes the state of the system and $\ProbI{i}$ concentrates dominant
mass on $a_t^{i\ast}$. The executed profile
$\tilde{A}_t=(\tilde a_t^K,\tilde a_t^S,\tilde a_t^F)$ is observable; the strategic
intention remains latent. The physical outcome is drawn with the competing law
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-given-tilde-A}
m_t \sim \ProbE\bigl(\cdot \mid \theta_K,\,\mathcal C_t\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal C_t$ incorporates competing risks, structural parameters, optimal
instruments, and materialized actions. Table~\ref{tab:mano-dios-soporte-mt} presents
three representative configurations of the support of $m_t$.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:mano-dios-filtro}
\noindent\textbf{MDG stochastic implementation filter.} Let $\mathcal{A}^i$ be the
finite set of admissible actions of agent $i$. Given the plan
$a_t^{i\ast}\in\mathcal{A}^i$ and state vector $X_t$, execution is determined by the
implementation law
\[
\tilde a_t^i\sim\ProbI{i}(\cdot\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t).
\]
The stochastic structure of the model is formalized on a measurable space
$(\Omega,\mathcal F)$, where $\Omega$ is the sample space of states of nature and
$\mathcal F$ the $\sigma$-algebra defining measurable events. Under this framework,
$\ProbI{i}$ assigns implementation probabilities, while the measurable function
$\mathcal H_t^i:\mathcal A^i\times\Omega\to\mathcal A^i$ materializes the sequential
draw, so that for each elementary event $\omega\in\Omega$ the executed action is
$\tilde a_t^i(\omega)=\mathcal H_t^i(a_t^{i\ast},\omega)$. This formulation makes the
aggregate profile of executed actions
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tilde-A-t}
\tilde{A}_t=(\tilde a_t^K,\tilde a_t^S,\tilde a_t^F)
\end{equation}
a random object that incorporates the operational perturbations of the period in a way
compatible with the underlying probability measure.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textup{(i)}] \textbf{Plan dominance and error support.} For all $t<\tau$, the
  distribution $\ProbI{i}$ concentrates most of its mass on $a_t^{i\ast}$, assigning
  strictly positive probability to alternative actions treated as \emph{implementation
  frictions}. Under \textbf{full support},
  $\ProbI{i}(\tilde a_t^i=a\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)>0$ for all $a\in\mathcal A^i$.
  \item[\textup{(ii)}] \textbf{Bounded institutional maturation.} The informational
  component $[H(\mu_t)/H(\mu_0)]e^{-\eta_{\mathrm{cal}}t}$ decreases as posterior
  uncertainty falls, while the structural floor $\underline c$ persists in
  \eqref{eq:temperatura-piso}. When $\underline c>0$, full support over
  $\mathcal A^i$ is preserved in the long run.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The implementation law $\ProbI{i}$ adopts a logit structure with hybrid temperature,
whose axiomatic justification is established in Remark~\ref{rem:axiomas-logit}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:logit-hybrid}
\ProbI{i}\bigl(\tilde{a}_t^i = a \bigm| a_t^{i\ast}, X_t\bigr)
=
\frac{\exp\bigl(\mathbf{1}\{a = a_t^{i\ast}\}/T_t\bigr)}
{\sum_{a'\in\mathcal A^i}\exp\bigl(\mathbf{1}\{a'=a_t^{i\ast}\}/T_t\bigr)}.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}[Axiomatic justification of the logit as the unique implementation law]
\label{rem:axiomas-logit}
Equation~\eqref{eq:logit-hybrid} is not an ad hoc choice: it is the \emph{unique}
distribution over $\mathcal A^i$ that simultaneously satisfies: \textup{(A1)}
\textbf{full support}, $\ProbI{i}(a\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)>0$ for all
$a\in\mathcal A^i$; \textup{(A2)} \textbf{dominance},
$\ProbI{i}(a_t^{i\ast}\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)>
\ProbI{i}(a\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)$ for all $a\neq a_t^{i\ast}$; and \textup{(A3)}
\textbf{maximum entropy}, among all distributions satisfying full support and
dominance. By the characterization theorem of
\textcite{Jaynes1957Information,Jaynes1957InformationII}, the entropy maximizer under
these restrictions is precisely the logit form in~\eqref{eq:logit-hybrid}.
\end{remark}

Here, $T_t=T_t(\mu_t,t)>0$ represents the \textbf{system temperature}; as long as it is
positive, law~\eqref{eq:logit-hybrid} maintains full support over $\mathcal A^i$ and
prevents likelihood degeneracy. To jointly capture strategic learning and institutional
maturation, define
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:temperatura-piso}
T_t=T_0\max\!\left\{\frac{H(\mu_t)}{H(\mu_0)}e^{-\eta_{\mathrm{cal}}t},\,\underline c\right\},
\end{equation}
where $T_0>0$ is initial temperature, $\eta_{\mathrm{cal}}>0$ the calendar decay rate,
$H(\mu_t)=-\sum_{\theta}\mu_t(\theta)\ln\mu_t(\theta)$ Shannon entropy, and
$\underline c\in[0,1]$ the structural noise floor.

\begin{definition}[Law of the physical outcome]
\label{def:desenlace-fisico-mt}
The \textbf{physical outcome} $m_t$ is an observable categorical variable on the support
\[
\mathcal M=\{\mathrm{Cont},1,2,3,4\},
\]
where $\mathrm{Cont}$ represents continuation of captivity at the close of the period
and $j\in\{1,2,3,4\}$ represents closure by competing cause $j$. Conditional on the
captor type and the period's material state,
\[
\mathcal C_t(\theta_K)=
\bigl(\theta_K,\theta_F,\theta_V,\theta_S,z,\tilde a_t^F,\tilde a_t^K,\tilde a_t^S,
\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast,p_{\mathrm{det},t}\bigr),
\]
its distribution is defined from effective intensities $\tilde\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_K)$.
The probability of continuation is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-law-cont}
\ProbE(m_t=\mathrm{Cont}\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)
=p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}
=\exp\left(-\sum_{j=1}^{4}\tilde\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_K)\Delta t\right).
\end{equation}
The total daily closure probability and each cause's relative weight are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-law-hazard}
q_t(\theta_K)=1-p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K},
\qquad
\xi_j(t\mid\theta_K)=
\frac{\tilde\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_K)}
{\sum_{\ell=1}^{4}\tilde\lambda_\ell(t\mid\theta_K)},
\qquad
h_j(t\mid\theta_K)=q_t(\theta_K)\xi_j(t\mid\theta_K).
\end{equation}
Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-law-out}
\ProbE(m_t=j\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)=h_j(t\mid\theta_K),
\qquad j=1,\dots,4.
\end{equation}
Materialization of $m_t$ is obtained by inverse transform. Let $v_t\sim\Unif(0,1)$ and
fix a total order on $\mathcal M$. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-law-inverse}
m_t=r
\iff
\sum_{\ell<r}\ProbE(m_t=\ell\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)
\le v_t <
\sum_{\ell\le r}\ProbE(m_t=\ell\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t).
\end{equation}
Thus, $m_t$ enters the Bayesian likelihood only once:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:m-law-likelihood}
\ProbE(m_t\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)
=
p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=\mathrm{Cont}\}}
\prod_{j=1}^{4}
h_j(t\mid\theta_K)^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=j\}}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Materialization Through Inverse Transform}
\label{sec:mecanismo-realizacion}

The model distinguishes between \textbf{strategic intention}, derived from the dynamic
program, and the \textbf{observed realization} in the data. This transition is
operationalized through a hierarchical inverse-transform draw. First,
$\mathcal H_t^i$ is the measurable materialization function of agent $i$, converting
the rational intention $a_t^{i\ast}$ and a uniform draw $u_{t,i}\sim\Unif(0,1)$ into an
executed action $\tilde a_t^i$ according to $\ProbI{i}$. Second, once the executed
triple $\tilde A_t$ is consolidated, the measurable rule $\mathcal G_t$ materializes
the physical outcome $m_t$ from material state $\mathcal C_t$ and an additional uniform
draw $v_t\sim\Unif(0,1)$ according to $\ProbE(\cdot\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)$. The
executed action is determined by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sorteo-individual}
\tilde{a}_t^i = a_k \iff
\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\ProbI{i}(a_j\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)
\le u_{t,i} <
\sum_{j=1}^{k}\ProbI{i}(a_j\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t).
\end{equation}
Given $\tilde A_t$ and $\mathcal C_t$, the final outcome is materialized within
$\mathcal M$ by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sorteo-m}
m_t = j \iff
\sum_{l<j}\ProbE(m_t=l\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)
\le v_t <
\sum_{l\le j}\ProbE(m_t=l\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Competing Risks and Survival}
\label{subsec:intensidades-prop}

Modeling the outcome of captivity begins by distinguishing between the
\textbf{instantaneous intensity} and its daily probabilistic projection, in line with
proportional hazards \textcite{Cox1972} and econometric treatments for interval
duration data \textcite{Greene2012,Wooldridge2010}. The \textbf{cause-specific
intensity} $\lambda_j(t)$ is the structural variable measuring the marginal propensity
for closure by cause $j$; as an instantaneous rate, it is measured in units of
$1/\mathrm{time}$ and is not bounded above by one. By contrast, for simulation and
daily observation, the \textbf{discrete hazard} $h_j(t)$ is a probability in $[0,1]$,
representing the likelihood that event $j$ materializes during interval $t$ conditional
on survival at the start of that period.

From a \textbf{material} perspective, risk intensities depend on the vector of
\textbf{executed} actions
$\tilde A_t=(\tilde a_t^F,\tilde a_t^K,\tilde a_t^S)$ generated by the MDG stochastic
process. The continuous instruments modifying hazards, detection, closure
probabilities, and likelihoods are the State's optima
$(\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)$, chosen by the control problem with informational
motive~\eqref{eq:state-dual-control-objective}.

For each cause $j\in\{1,2,3,4\}$, let $\lambda_{j0}(t)$ be the baseline intensity and
let $z$ denote the vector of observables. Effective intensity is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hj}
\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_K)
=
\lambda_{j0}(t)
\exp\!\left(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_K)\cdot z
+\zeta_{\alpha,j}\alpha_t^\ast
+\zeta_{\gamma,j}\gamma_t^\ast\right).
\end{equation}
The maturation filter, payment/continuation probability, and relative cause weights
are
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:maturation-filter}
M(t):=\min\!\left\{1,\left(\frac{t}{T_{\mathrm{mad}}}\right)^2\right\},
\end{equation}
\begin{align}
\label{eq:pCont}
p_{\mathrm{Cont},t}
&=\exp\!\left(-\sum_{j=1}^{4}\tilde{\lambda}_j(t)\Delta t\right),\\
\label{eq:q}
q(t)&=1-p_{\mathrm{Cont},t},
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xi}
\xi_j(t)=
\frac{\tilde{\lambda}_j(t)}
{\sum_{\ell=1}^{4}\tilde{\lambda}_{\ell}(t)}, 
\quad j=1,\dots,4,
\quad \text{where } \sum_{j=1}^{4}\xi_j(t)=1.
\end{equation}
If the sum of effective intensities is zero, $q(t)=0$ and no terminal cause is drawn.
The daily marginal probability of closure by cause $j$ is then
$h_j(t)=q(t)\xi_j(t)$, so that $\sum_j h_j(t)=q(t)\le1$ by construction.

\subsubsection{Survival Under Focal Rescue, Bayesian Accuracy, and Human Loss}
\label{subsec:rescate-survival-precision}

At the beginning of each period $t$, the State observes the public information flow
$\mathcal I_t^S$ and updates its posterior belief
$\mu_t(\theta_K)=\ProbE(\theta_K\mid\mathcal I_t^S)$ by Bayesian recursion. To
operationalize information quality in rescue planning, define the precision index
$\iota_t$ as the \textbf{probability mass of the modal type}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:iota-precision-mode}
\iota_t:=\max_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta),
\quad
\text{with estimated profile }
\hat{\theta}_t=\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta).
\end{equation}
Under this scheme, $\iota_t$ acts as an \textbf{operational confidence index}. However,
rescue effectiveness depends not only on posterior concentration but also on the
truthfulness of identification. Conditional on a focal rescue attempt and on the true
type $\theta_K$, survival probability is modeled through a logistic response penalizing
miscalibration between the estimated and true profiles:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p-surv-rescue-logit-ajustado}
\ProbE(s_t=1\mid\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta_K)
=
\Lambda\Bigl(\alpha_{\mathrm{leth}}(\theta_K)
+\beta_R\,\iota_t\,\mathbf{1}\{\hat{\theta}_t=\theta_K\}\Bigr).
\end{equation}
If the State correctly identifies the captor, the intelligence input $\iota_t$
enhances the probability of success according to productivity $\beta_R$; if
identification is wrong, accumulated intelligence becomes ineffective or miscalibrated,
reducing survival probability to the base level determined by intrinsic lethality
$\alpha_{\mathrm{leth}}(\theta_K)$.

This subsection projects the daily decision mechanism into a \textbf{longitudinal
framework}, establishing the mapping between effective intensities
$\tilde\lambda_j(t)$, marginal risks $h_j(t)$, and continuation probability
$p_{\mathrm{Cont},t}$ with historical duration and closure-mode records. Let $S(0)=1$.
For every $t\ge1$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:longitudinal-system}
S(t)=\prod_{t'=1}^{t}p_{\mathrm{Cont},t'},
\quad
F_j(t)=\sum_{t'=1}^{t}h_j(t')S(t'-1),
\quad
\text{with } S(t)+\sum_{j=1}^{4}F_j(t)=1.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Expected captivity time and operational pressure]
\label{prop:expected-captivity-gamma}
\label{prop:duracion-presion}
Define the \textbf{net sensitivity} of type $\theta_K$ to optimal operational pressure
$\gamma_t^\ast$ as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kappa-c}
\kappa_h(\theta_K,t):=
\left(\zeta_{\gamma,2}\tilde{\lambda}_2(t\mid\theta_K)
+\zeta_{\gamma,3}\tilde{\lambda}_3(t\mid\theta_K)\right)
-\zeta_{\gamma,1}\tilde{\lambda}_1(t\mid\theta_K).
\end{equation}
If $\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)>0$ for all $t$, the expected captivity time
$\mathbb E[\tau\mid\theta_K]$ is strictly decreasing in $\gamma_t^\ast$. If
$\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)<0$ for all $t$, it is strictly increasing. The model therefore
generates the \textbf{testable differential prediction}
\[
\operatorname{sgn}\left(
\frac{\partial\mathbb E[\tau\mid\theta_K]}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast}
\right)
=-\operatorname{sgn}(\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The proof follows three steps: differentiating daily survival, propagating the
derivative through the product rule, and concluding by dominated convergence.
By Fubini's theorem, the expectation of a non-negative integer-valued random variable
can be written as the sum of its survival function:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:duracion-identidad}
\mathbb{E}[\tau\mid\theta_K]
=\sum_{t'=0}^{\infty}P(\tau>t'\mid\theta_K)
=\sum_{t'=0}^{\infty}S(t'\mid\theta_K).
\end{equation}
Since only $\Lambda_t$ depends on $\gamma_t^\ast$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dpCont-dgamma}
\frac{\partial p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast}
=
-\Bigl(\sum_{j=1}^{3}\zeta_{\gamma,j}\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_K)\Bigr)
p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}\Delta t.
\end{equation}
Regrouping terms by whether the channel accelerates or delays closure yields the sign
summarized by $\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)$. The derivative of survival is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dS-dgamma}
\frac{\partial S(t'\mid\theta_K)}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast}
=
S(t'\mid\theta_K)
\frac{1}{p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}}
\frac{\partial p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta_K}}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast},
\qquad t\le t',
\end{equation}
and zero for $t>t'$. Therefore,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dE-dgamma}
\frac{\partial\mathbb E[\tau\mid\theta_K]}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast}
=
\sum_{t'=t}^{\infty}
\frac{\partial S(t'\mid\theta_K)}{\partial\gamma_t^\ast},
\end{equation}
whose sign is the opposite of $\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)$ under the stated uniform sign
condition.
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Capture Probability}
\label{subsec:prob-captura}

The probability of capture or detection is governed by operational pressure and by the
institutional environment. In reduced form,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p-cap-def}
p_{\mathrm{cap},t}
=\ProbE(d_t=1\mid\theta_K,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast,z,\theta_S),
\end{equation}
with technical specification
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p-cap-tecnica}
p_{\mathrm{cap},t}
=
\Lambda\!\left(\rho_0+\rho_\gamma\gamma_t^\ast+\rho_\alpha\alpha_t^\ast
+\rho_z z+\rho_S\theta_S-\rho_K(\theta_K)\right).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Physical Likelihood}
\label{subsec:verosimilitud-fisica}

The physical likelihood separates continuation and terminal-outcome cases:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LH-cont}
\mathcal L_{F,t}(\theta\mid m_t=\mathrm{Cont})
=p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta}^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=\mathrm{Cont}\}},
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LH-out}
\mathcal L_{F,t}(\theta\mid m_t=j)
=h_j(t\mid\theta)^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=j\}},
\end{equation}
and compactly,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LH-compacta}
\mathcal L_{F,t}(\theta)
=
p_{\mathrm{Cont},t\mid\theta}^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=\mathrm{Cont}\}}
\prod_{j=1}^{4}h_j(t\mid\theta)^{\mathbf 1\{m_t=j\}}.
\end{equation}
The detection likelihood is Bernoulli:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Ld-bernoulli}
\mathcal L_{d,t}(\theta)
=p_{\mathrm{cap},t}(\theta)^{d_t}\bigl(1-p_{\mathrm{cap},t}(\theta)\bigr)^{1-d_t}.
\end{equation}
The executed-action likelihood induced by the MDG layer is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LI-atilde}
\mathcal L_{I,t}(\theta)
=\prod_{i\in\{K,S,F\}}\ProbI{i}\bigl(\tilde a_t^i\mid a_t^{i\ast}(\theta),X_t\bigr),
\end{equation}
so the joint physical likelihood is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LH-joint}
\mathcal L_{F,t}^{\mathrm{joint}}(\theta)
=\mathcal L_{I,t}(\theta)\mathcal L_{F,t}(\theta)\mathcal L_{d,t}(\theta).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Voice}
\label{subsec:voz}

The communication channel is decomposed as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:voz-descomp}
\mathcal L_{C,t}(\theta)=\ProbE(c_t\mid\theta,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast,d_t),
\end{equation}
with full likelihood
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Lvoz-full}
\mathcal L_{C,t}(\theta)
=\pi_{\mathrm{call}}(d_t,\gamma_t^\ast,\theta)^{c_t}
\bigl(1-\pi_{\mathrm{call}}(d_t,\gamma_t^\ast,\theta)\bigr)^{1-c_t},
\end{equation}
and diagonal shorthand
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Lvoz-diag}
\pi_{\mathrm{call}}(d_t,\gamma_t^\ast,\theta)
=\Lambda(\delta_\theta+\zeta_{\gamma,\theta}\gamma_t^\ast+\zeta_d d_t).
\end{equation}
The combined communication likelihood is denoted
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LC}
\mathcal L_{C,t}(\theta).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Bayesian Updating}
\label{sec:bayesian-updating}

The full likelihood used by the mechanism is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:LF-joint-opt}
\mathcal L_t(\theta)
=\mathcal L_{F,t}^{\mathrm{joint}}(\theta)\mathcal L_{C,t}(\theta),
\end{equation}
and the posterior update is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bayes-unif}
\mu_{t+1}(\theta)
=
\frac{\mu_t(\theta)\mathcal L_t(\theta)}
{\sum_{\theta'\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta')\mathcal L_t(\theta')}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Agents: Optimization and Equilibrium}
\label{sec:actions}
\label{sec:mapa-acciones}

In this way, the mechanism is structured under a \textbf{Principal-Agent} architecture in a dynamic environment. The \textbf{State ($S$)} acts as the designer (\textit{Principal}) that seeks to align the choices of the \textbf{Agents} (Kidnapper and Family) toward the termination of the event.\footnote{%
\textbf{Probability and utility convention.} Unless another measure is explicitly indicated \textup{(}e.g., $\mathbb{Q}$ in martingale definitions\textup{)}, $\ProbE(\cdot)$ and $\E[\cdot]$ denote probabilities and expectations under the ex ante law of the mechanism; $\ProbI{i}$ denotes player $i$'s stochastic implementation law \textup{(}MDG process\textup{)}; $\ProbC{\theta}$ denotes the \textbf{counterfactual} episode law that fixes $\theta_K=\theta$. In the utility functions $\mathcal{U}_t^{i}$, the conditional probabilities that weight outcomes are written with $\ProbE(\cdot\mid\cdot)$ unless the context explicitly indicates $\ProbI{i}$ or $\ProbC{\theta}$. The continuous uniform distribution on $[0,1]$ is denoted $\Unif(0,1)$, separately from utilities $\mathcal{U}$. Primitive scalar probabilities by type \textup{(}e.g., contact intensity\textup{)} are written $\pi_{\mathrm{call}}(\theta_K)$ so as not to confuse them with $\ProbE$, $\ProbI{i}$, $\ProbC{\theta}$, or with the action label $\mathrm{P}$ in operational tables.}

\subsubsection[Analysis of kidnapper K]{Analysis of the Kidnapper ($K$), Origin and Maximization of Actions}
In each period $t$, the captor solves an optimal stopping problem by selecting $a^K_t\in\mathcal{A}^K=\{a_{rel},a_{kill},a_{cont}\}$ that maximizes its value function:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kidnapper-argmax}
    a_t^{K\ast} = \arg \max_{a^K_t \in \mathcal{A}^K} \left\{ \mathcal{U}^K_t(a^K_t \mid h_t, \theta_K, \theta_S) \right\}
\end{equation}

\noindent \textbf{Death ($a_{kill}$).} The return from executing the hostage weighs the reputational benefit $\eta(\theta_i)$ against the expected penalty $\tilde{p}_{cap,t}(\theta_i)F_{\mathrm{cap}}(\theta_i,\theta_S)$ if the captor is intercepted at the margin of the period\footnote{\textbf{Conditioning.} In all branches, the effective probabilities $\tilde p_{\cdot,t}$ are defined as conditional expectations over the MDG realizations induced by the relevant tuple $\mathcal Q_t$. The explicit expressions for $\tilde p_{cap,t}$, $\tilde p_{pay,t}$, $\tilde p_{surv,t}$, $\tilde p_{rel,t}$, and $\tilde p_{kill,t}$ are reported below in the equations for the kidnapper, the family, and the State.}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kidnapper-kill}
\begin{split}
U^K_{kill}(\theta_i, \theta_S)
={}& \Bigl(1-\E_{\tilde a_t^S\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}}\!\bigl[
      p_{cap}(\tilde a_t^S,\theta_i,\theta_S,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)
    \bigr]\Bigr)\eta(\theta_i) \\
&{}-\E_{\tilde a_t^S\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}}\!\bigl[
      p_{cap}(\tilde a_t^S,\theta_i,\theta_S,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)
    \bigr]F_{\mathrm{cap}}(\theta_i,\theta_S).
\end{split}
\end{equation}

\noindent \textbf{Continuation ($a_{cont}$).} The captor's Bellman equation combines the current flow---expected ransom net of blocking, operational cost, and capture penalty---with the type-discounted future value. Since $\theta_K$ is the captor's private information, $\mu_t$ is not uncertainty for him but rather a strategic object: the evolution of $\mu_{t+1}$ conditions the State's future policy.

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:kidnapper-cont}
  \begin{aligned}
  V^K_{cont,t}
  &=
    \E_{\tilde A_t\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cont}}}\!\Bigl[
      \ProbE(m_t=\mathrm{pay}\mid \tilde A_t,X_t',\theta_K=\theta_i)
    \Bigr] R\, (1 - \alpha_t^\ast) \\
  &\quad - C_t(\gamma_t^\ast, \theta_i) \\
  &\quad - \E_{\tilde a_t^S\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}}\!\Bigl[
      p_{cap}(\tilde a_t^S,\theta_i,\theta_S,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)
    \Bigr] F_{\mathrm{cap}}(\theta_i,\theta_S) \\
  &\quad +
    \beta(\theta_i)\,\Bigl(1-\E_{\tilde a_t^S\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}}\!\bigl[
      p_{cap}(\tilde a_t^S,\theta_i,\theta_S,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)
    \bigr]\Bigr)\,
    \E_{\theta_i}\!\left[
      V^K_{t+1}\!\left(\mu_{t+1}(a_{cont})\right)
    \right].
  \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\noindent where the captor's effective probabilities are written as
\begin{align}
\tilde{p}_{cap,t}(\theta_i)
&:= \E_{\tilde a_t^S\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}}\!\Bigl[
    p_{cap}(\tilde a_t^S,\theta_i,\theta_S,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)
  \Bigr], \\
\tilde{p}_{pay,t}(\theta_i)
&:= \E_{\tilde A_t\mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cont}}}\!\Bigl[
    \ProbE(m_t=\mathrm{pay}\mid \tilde A_t,X_t',\theta_K=\theta_i)
  \Bigr],
\end{align}
with
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cap}}
&:=
\bigl(a_t^{S\ast},\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\theta_K,\theta_S\bigr), \\
\mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Cont}}
&:=
\bigl(a_t^{S\ast},a_t^{F\ast},a_{\mathrm{cont}},\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mu_t,\theta_K\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]

The convex-exponential specification~\eqref{eq:cost-function-kidnapper} is designed to induce a separating equilibrium. The parameter $\kappa_c(\theta_i)>0$ generates the \textit{single-crossing property}: variations in $\gamma_t$ affect different types $\theta_i$ asymmetrically, turning the persistence or termination of captivity into an informative signal for the State's Bayesian updating.

\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:cost-function-kidnapper}
  C_t(\gamma_t, \theta_i) = \phi(\theta_i)\,\exp\bigl( \kappa_c(\theta_i)\,\gamma_t \bigr) + \nu(\theta_i)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Release ($a_{rel}$).} This branch collects the utility associated with the strategic closure of the kidnapping without obtaining rent. Let $\kappa_{rel}(\theta_i)\ge 0$ be the parameter, or function, that represents the disutility tied to release, reflected in reputational loss or foregone rent. By contrast, $\eta(\theta_i)$ summarizes the reputational benefit, or other private arrangements of the captor, when he materializes $a_{kill}$ without being captured at the margin of $t$. Under this notation, release utility is expressed as
\[
U^K_{rel}(\theta_i) = -\kappa_{rel}(\theta_i).
\]

\noindent Compactly, the kidnapper's utility can be written as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kidnapper-utility-general}
\mathcal{U}^K_t(a_t^K \mid h_t,\theta_K,\theta_S)
=
\begin{cases}
U^K_{rel}(\theta_i), & \textit{if } a_t^K = a_{rel},\\
U^K_{kill}(\theta_i,\theta_S), & \textit{if } a_t^K = a_{kill},\\
V^K_{cont,t}, & \textit{if } a_t^K = a_{cont}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

In the numerical laboratory---where $m_t$ is not observable---$K$'s program is closed with an \textbf{intrinsic stopping rule}: in each $t$, $V^K_{\mathrm{cont},t}$ is contrasted with the terminal utilities of release and killing until continuation no longer dominates the best \emph{one-shot} payoff. It is useful to distinguish three objects: \textbf{(i)}~the \textbf{physical} stopping time $\tau$ in~\eqref{eq:stopping-time}, the anchor of the observable episode and basis of $\mathbb{E}[\tau\mid\theta_K]$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:expected-captivity-gamma}; \textbf{(ii)}~the integer that bounds $\mathbb{T}=\{0,1,\ldots,\tau\}$ when formalizing the mechanism, merely a calendar \emph{ceiling} with no mechanical link to~\eqref{eq:stopping-time}; and \textbf{(iii)}~the simulated truncation rule stated below. We denote
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tau-K-pure-simulation}
\tau_K^{\mathrm{sim}}
\;:=\;
\min\Bigl\{\, t\ge 0 : V^K_{\mathrm{cont},t} \le \max\bigl\{-\kappa_{\mathrm{rel}}(\theta_K),\,U^K_{\mathrm{kill}}(\theta_K,\theta_S)\bigr\} \Bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $-\kappa_{\mathrm{rel}}(\theta_K)=U^K_{\mathrm{rel}}(\theta_K)$ in the $a_{\mathrm{rel}}$ branch and $U^K_{\mathrm{kill}}$ appears in~\eqref{eq:kidnapper-kill}. The symbol $\tau_K^{\mathrm{sim}}$ summarizes the first margin at which continuation no longer exceeds the maximum between surrender and lethality; it serves as a \textbf{truncation criterion} in simulation. The $\tau$ in~\eqref{eq:stopping-time}, by contrast, marks when the physical outcome absorbs the episode in $\mathcal{M}$.

\subsubsection[The family F]{The Family ($F$), Rational Maximization of Survival}
In each period $t$, the family chooses $a_t^F\in\mathcal{A}^F=\{a_{\mathrm{coop}},a_{\mathrm{col}}\}$ by solving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:family-argmax-bayes}
    a_t^{F\ast}
    \in \arg \max_{a^F \in \{a_{\mathrm{coop}},\, a_{\mathrm{col}}\}}
    \E_{\theta_K \mid \mathcal{I}_t^F}
    \left[
    \mathcal{U}^F_t(a^F \mid \theta_K, \theta_F)
    \right].
\end{equation}

\noindent \textbf{Cooperation ($a_{\mathrm{coop}}$).} The family cooperates when the expected value of the victim's survival, weighted by $V_L>0$, exceeds the cost of following the institutional route $e_t(\gamma_t,\theta_F)$:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:family-utility-coop}
    \mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{coop}}\mid \theta_K,\theta_F)
    =
    \E_{\theta_K \mid \mathcal{I}_t^F}
    \left[
    \E_{\tilde A_t \mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Coop}}}\!\Bigl[
      \ProbE\bigl(s_t=1\mid \gamma_t,\tilde A_t,\theta_K\bigr)
    \Bigr]
    \right] V_L
    - e_t(\gamma_t,\theta_F).
\end{equation}

\noindent The institutional cost adopts the \textbf{exponential-convex} form, analogous to the captor's $C_t(\gamma_t,\theta_i)$ in~\eqref{eq:cost-function-kidnapper}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:family-institutional-cost}
    e_t(\gamma_t,\theta_F)
    \;=\;
    \phi_F(\theta_F)\,\exp\bigl(\kappa_F(\theta_F)\,\gamma_t\bigr)
    \;+\;
    \nu_F(\theta_F),
\end{equation}
with $\phi_F(\theta_F)>0$ and $\kappa_F(\theta_F)>0$ for every $\theta_F\in\Theta_F=\{\text{High wealth},\text{Low wealth}\}$, and $\nu_F(\theta_F)\in\mathbb{R}$ a type-specific fixed shift.\footnote{As with $\kappa_c(\theta_K)$ in~\eqref{eq:cost-function-kidnapper}, the exponent $\kappa_F(\theta_F)$ governs marginal sensitivity to operational hardening: families with higher $\kappa_F$ suffer a sharper increase in the cost of cooperation when $\gamma_t$ grows, generating \textit{single-crossing} in $\gamma_t$ if $\kappa_F$ varies with $\theta_F$. In that case, the cooperation path ceases to be neutral with respect to $\Theta_F$ and may become informative about the family's type, opening a \textbf{second dimension} of Bayesian learning complementary to the identification of $\theta_K$. When heterogeneity in $F$ is not an object of inference, $\kappa_F$ and $\phi_F$ may be taken as constant in $\theta_F$ without loss of parsimony.}

\noindent where
\[
\begin{aligned}
\tilde p_{\mathrm{surv},t}(\theta_i)
&:=
\E_{\tilde A_t \mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Coop}}}\!\Bigl[
\ProbE\bigl(s_t=1\mid \gamma_t,\tilde A_t,\theta_K=\theta_i\bigr)
\Bigr], \\
\mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Coop}}
&:=
\bigl(a_t^{S\ast},a_{\mathrm{coop}},a_t^{K\ast},\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mu_t,\theta_K\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]

\noindent \textbf{Collusion ($a_{\mathrm{col}}$).} The family colludes by clandestinely paying the ransom $R$; utility discounts the probability of detection and the fine $F_{\mathrm{col}}$:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:family-utility-col}
    \begin{split}
    \mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{col}})
    &=
    \E_{\theta_K \mid \mathcal{I}_t^F}
    \biggl[
    \E_{\tilde A_t \mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Col}}}\!\Bigl[
      \ProbE\bigl(m_t=\mathrm{rel}\mid \tilde A_t,R,\theta_K\bigr)
    \Bigr]
    \biggr] V_L \\
    &\quad - R
    -\E_{\tilde{a}_t^F\mid\QtinfoF}\Bigl[
    \ProbE\bigl(d_t{=}1\mid\alpha_t,\tilde{a}_t^F,\mathcal{I}_t^F\bigr)
    \Bigr]F_{\mathrm{col}}.
    \end{split}
\end{equation}

\noindent where
\[
\begin{aligned}
\tilde p_{\mathrm{rel},t}(\theta_i)
&:=
\E_{\tilde A_t \mid \mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Col}}}\!\Bigl[
\ProbE\bigl(m_t=\mathrm{rel}\mid \tilde A_t,R,\theta_K=\theta_i\bigr)
\Bigr], \\
\mathcal Q_t^{\mathrm{Col}}
&:=
\bigl(a_t^{S\ast},a_{\mathrm{col}},a_t^{K\ast},\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mu_t,\theta_K\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\]

\noindent Compactly,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:family-utility-general}
\mathcal{U}^F_t(a_t^F \mid \theta_K,\theta_F)
=
\begin{cases}
\mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{coop}}), & \textit{if } a_t^F = a_{\mathrm{coop}},\\
\mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{col}}), & \textit{if } a_t^F = a_{\mathrm{col}}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection[The State S]{The State ($S$), Mechanism Design and Optimal Decision}
\label{sec:state-mechanism}

In each period $t$, given the accumulated public history $\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}$ and the belief vector $\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$, the State $S$ solves a Bayesian dual-control problem. Its choice combines a discrete institutional decision,
$a_t^S\in\{a_{\mathrm{res}},a_{\mathrm{neg}}\}$, and two continuous instruments, $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in[0,1]^2$, where $\alpha_t$ measures financial blocking and $\gamma_t$ operational pressure. The decision is taken with the modal type $\hat{\theta}_t$ and informational precision $\iota_t$ defined in~\eqref{eq:iota-precision-mode}. The objective is not to maximize a utility of the State, but to minimize an expected social loss that combines human risk, economic costs, operational costs, and the value of learning.

\paragraph{General problem of the State.}
The admissible set $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ incorporates institutional feasibility and the belief-weighted incentive compatibility and participation constraints \textup{(}$IC_t^K$, $IR_t^K$, $IR_t^F$\textup{)}. The executable problem of the State is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-dual-control-objective}
\begin{aligned}
(a_t^{S*},\alpha_t^*,\gamma_t^*)
&\in
\arg\min_{(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\Gamma_t(\mu_t)}
\left\{
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)
L_t(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\theta,\iota_t)
+ 
\Pi_{t,a^S}^{S}(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t)
-\psi_H\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
\right\}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The first term is the expected material loss; $\Pi_{t,a^S}^{S}$ disciplines how far instruments depart from a Bayesian anchor by branch; and $-\psi_H\Delta H_t$ introduces the informational motive. Given the mixed nature of the choice, the solution is obtained by partition: first, the continuous instruments are minimized within each branch $b\in\{R,N\}$; then the optimal rescue and negotiation values are compared.

\paragraph{Objective functions under rescue and negotiation.}
The instantaneous loss conditional on the latent type is defined by branch:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-loss-conditional-branches}
L_t(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\theta_K,\iota_t)=
\begin{cases}
V_t^R(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta_K,\alpha_t,\gamma_t), & \text{if } a_t^S=a_{\mathrm{res}},\\[4pt]
V_t^N(\theta_K,\alpha_t,\gamma_t), & \text{if } a_t^S=a_{\mathrm{neg}}.
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Under tactical rescue, the State minimizes the expected cost of lethal failure of the operation plus the deployment cost:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-loss-rescue-branch}
V_t^R(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta_K,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
:=
\omega_k\left[
1-\ProbE\!\left(s_t=1\mid \iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta_K\right)
\right]
+ 
C_{\mathrm{ops}}(\gamma_t,\alpha_t;\theta_K).
\end{equation}
Here $\ProbE(s_t=1\mid\cdot)$ is the probability of survival under rescue, specified in~\eqref{eq:p-surv-rescue-logit-ajustado}; therefore, lower precision $\iota_t$ makes branch $R$ more costly because of greater human risk.

Under negotiation, the State minimizes the expected cost of criminal transfer net of blocking, captivity lethality, and maintenance of the cordon:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-loss-neg-branch}
V_t^N(\theta_K,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
:=
\omega_pR(1-\alpha_t)
+\omega_k h_2(t\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)
+C_{\mathrm{maint}}(\gamma_t,\alpha_t;\theta_K).
\end{equation}
The rate $h_2(t\mid\theta_K,\mathcal C_t)$ corresponds to the daily death hazard derived from the competing-risks system~\eqref{eq:xi}. Under uncertainty, the expected value of the negotiation branch is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:negotiation-cost}
V_t^N(\mu_t,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
:=
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)
\left[
\omega_pR(1-\alpha_t)
+\omega_k h_2(t\mid\theta,\mathcal C_t)
+C_{\mathrm{maint}}(\gamma_t,\alpha_t;\theta)
\right].
\end{equation}

The optimized floors of each branch are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rescue-cost}
\begin{aligned}
V_t^{R,*}(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\mu_t)
&=
\min_{(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\Gamma_t^R(\mu_t)}
\left\{
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)
V_t^R(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
+\Pi_{t,R}^{S}(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t)
-\psi_H\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
\right\},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:negotiation-piso}
\begin{aligned}
V_t^{N,*}(\mu_t)
&=
\min_{(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\Gamma_t^N(\mu_t)}
\left\{
V_t^N(\mu_t,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
+\Pi_{t,N}^{S}(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t)
-\psi_H\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
\right\}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Operational cost components.}
The functions $C_{\mathrm{ops}}$ and $C_{\mathrm{maint}}$ are quadratic, continuous, and convex in $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$. Their parameters depend on type $\theta_K$ to capture logistical, territorial, and financial heterogeneity:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cops-quadratic}
C_{\mathrm{ops}}(\gamma_t,\alpha_t;\theta_K)
=
c_0(\theta_K)+c_1(\theta_K)\gamma_t+\frac{c_2(\theta_K)}{2}\gamma_t^2
+c_3(\theta_K)\alpha_t+\frac{c_4(\theta_K)}{2}\alpha_t^2
+c_5(\theta_K)\gamma_t\alpha_t,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cmaint-quadratic}
C_{\mathrm{maint}}(\gamma_t,\alpha_t;\theta_K)
=
m_0(\theta_K)+m_1(\theta_K)\gamma_t+\frac{m_2(\theta_K)}{2}\gamma_t^2
+m_3(\theta_K)\alpha_t+\frac{m_4(\theta_K)}{2}\alpha_t^2
+m_5(\theta_K)\gamma_t\alpha_t.
\end{equation}
The cross terms $c_5(\theta_K)$ and $m_5(\theta_K)$ capture complementarity or substitutability between financial blocking and operational pressure.

\paragraph{Perfect-information anchors and deviation function.}
To prevent the State from choosing arbitrarily extreme instruments under uncertainty, counterfactual references are constructed by type and branch. These references are not executable policies when the type is unknown; they are anchors indicating what the State would do if it knew $\theta$ with certainty:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:benchmark-rescue-type}
(\alpha_{t,R}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta),\gamma_{t,R}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta))
\in
\arg\min_{(\alpha,\gamma)\in[0,1]^2}
V_t^R(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta,\alpha,\gamma),
\qquad \forall\theta\in\Theta_K,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:benchmark-neg-type}
(\alpha_{t,N}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta),\gamma_{t,N}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta))
\in
\arg\min_{(\alpha,\gamma)\in[0,1]^2}
V_t^N(\theta,\alpha,\gamma),
\qquad \forall\theta\in\Theta_K.
\end{equation}
The loss frontiers under perfect information are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:benchmark-perfect-info}
\begin{aligned}
B_{t,R}(\mu_t)
&:=
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)
V_t^R\!\left(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\theta,
\alpha_{t,R}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta),
\gamma_{t,R}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta)\right),\\
B_{t,N}(\mu_t)
&:=
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)
V_t^N\!\left(\theta,
\alpha_{t,N}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta),
\gamma_{t,N}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta)\right).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The Bayesian center of branch $b\in\{R,N\}$ is obtained by weighting those references by the current belief:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:belief-weighted-policy}
\alpha_{t,b}^{\mu}
:=
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)\alpha_{t,b}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta),
\qquad
\gamma_{t,b}^{\mu}
:=
\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)\gamma_{t,b}^{\mathrm{bench}}(\theta).
\end{equation}
The deviation penalty measures how far the candidate policy moves away from that Bayesian center:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:prima-desviacion}
\Pi_{t,b}^S(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t)
=
\chi_\alpha(\alpha_t-\alpha_{t,b}^{\mu})^2
+\chi_\gamma(\gamma_t-\gamma_{t,b}^{\mu})^2,
\qquad b\in\{R,N\},\quad \chi_\alpha,\chi_\gamma\ge 0.
\end{equation}
Thus, $\Pi_{t,b}^S$ fulfills a regulatory function: it allows exploration, but charges a premium when the instrument departs from the Bayesian center that summarizes the perfect-information references.

\paragraph{Role of Shannon entropy.}
Entropy introduces the active exploration motive. The State recognizes that $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$ modifies the Cox intensities and the outcome probabilities of Table 10; therefore, a policy can be valuable not only because it reduces material loss today, but because it separates likelihoods and reduces uncertainty tomorrow. Belief entropy is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:belief-entropy}
H(\mu_t)
:=
-\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)\log\mu_t(\theta),
\end{equation}
with the convention $0\log0=0$. For each pair of public signals $(m,d)$ with $m\in\{\mathrm{Cont},\mathrm{rel},\mathrm{res},\mathrm{pay},\mathrm{kill}\}$ and $d\in\{0,1\}$, the State computes the counterfactual posterior that would result from observing them under the candidate policy:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:posterior-counterfactual-entropy}
\mu_{t+1}^{m,d}(\theta)
=
\frac{
\mu_t(\theta)\ProbE(m_t=m\mid\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mathcal C_t)\ProbE(d_t=d\mid\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
}{
\sum_{\theta'\in\Theta_K}
\mu_t(\theta')\ProbE(m_t=m\mid\theta',\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mathcal C_t)\ProbE(d_t=d\mid\theta',\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
}.
\end{equation}
The expected information gain is the expected reduction of entropy over the joint public signal $(m_t,d_t)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:expected-information-gain}
\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
=
H(\mu_t)
-
\sum_{m}\sum_{d\in\{0,1\}}
\ProbE(m_t=m,d_t=d\mid\mu_t,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mathcal C_t)
H(\mu_{t+1}^{m,d}),
\end{equation}
where the joint predictive probability factorizes by conditional independence given the type: $\ProbE(m,d\mid\mu_t,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\mathcal C_t)=\sum_{\theta}\mu_t(\theta)\ProbE(m_t=m\mid\theta,\mathcal C_t)\ProbE(d_t=d\mid\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$. The term $-\psi_H\Delta H_t$ operates as an informational subsidy: if $\psi_H=0$, the State is purely myopic; if $\psi_H>0$, policies that buy learning receive a discount in the loss. When $\mu_t$ concentrates on the true type, $H(\mu_t)$ and $\Delta H_t$ fall, endogenously extinguishing the exploratory motive.

\paragraph{Discrete decision rule and equilibrium.}
Once the two floors have been calculated, the State compares:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-expected-loss}
\mathcal L_t^{S*}
=
\min\left\{
V_t^{R,*}(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\mu_t),
V_t^{N,*}(\mu_t)
\right\}.
\end{equation}
If $(\alpha_{\mathrm{res}}^*,\gamma_{\mathrm{res}}^*)$ and $(\alpha_{\mathrm{neg}}^*,\gamma_{\mathrm{neg}}^*)$ denote the continuous argminima of branches $R$ and $N$, the executed policy is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:state-discrete-rule}
(a_t^{S*},\alpha_t^*,\gamma_t^*)
=
\begin{cases}
(a_{\mathrm{res}},\alpha_{\mathrm{res}}^*,\gamma_{\mathrm{res}}^*),
& \text{if } V_t^{R,*}(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\mu_t)\le V_t^{N,*}(\mu_t),\\[6pt]
(a_{\mathrm{neg}},\alpha_{\mathrm{neg}}^*,\gamma_{\mathrm{neg}}^*),
& \text{if } V_t^{R,*}(\iota_t,\hat{\theta}_t,\mu_t)>V_t^{N,*}(\mu_t).
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the case of strict numerical equivalence between the loss floors, rescue prevalence is assumed by the weak inequality in the first condition.

\subsection{Incentive Compatibility and Individual Rationality Constraints}
\label{subsec:ir-ic}

The State's loss minimization is bounded by three design constraints.

\noindent\textbf{Bayesian incentive compatibility for the kidnapper ($IC^K$).}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ic-kidnapper}
\mathbb{E}_{\theta\sim\mu_t}
\left[V^K_t(a^*(\theta)\mid\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
-V^K_t(a^*(\theta_j)\mid\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\right]\ge0,
\quad\forall\,\theta_j\in\Theta_K.
\end{equation}
The expectation is taken over the true type; $\theta_j$ indexes a fixed mimicry
deviation. When $\mu_t(\theta^*)\to1$, this collapses to standard incentive
compatibility of the true type against each rival imitation.

\noindent\textbf{Bayesian participation constraint for the kidnapper ($IR^K$)---deterrence.}
This constraint operates under a punitive logic of strategic deterrence. It requires
the utility from bloodless release to dominate the best criminal alternative:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ir-K}
\mathbb{E}_{\theta\sim\mu_t}
\left[U^K_{\mathrm{rel}}(\theta)
-\max\!\left\{V^K_{\mathrm{cont},t}(\theta,\alpha_t,\gamma_t),
U^K_{\mathrm{kill}}(\theta,\theta_S)\right\}\right]\ge0.
\end{equation}
This is consistent with the logic of \textcite{Becker1968} adapted to armed conflict:
public policy erodes the expected profitability of persisting or escalating violence
relative to surrender.

\noindent\textbf{Family participation constraint ($IR^F$).}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ir-family}
\mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{coop}})\ge\mathcal{U}^F_t(a_{\mathrm{col}}).
\end{equation}
The feasible policy set simultaneously satisfying all three constraints is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma-factible}
\Gamma_t(\mu_t)
:=\bigl\{(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\mathcal{A}^{S,\mathrm{disc}}\times[0,1]^2:
IC^K,\;IR^K,\;IR^F\text{ are satisfied}\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
Its non-emptiness---\textbf{robust feasibility}---is the primitive condition of the
implementability theorem.

\section{Bayesian Learning and Type Identification}
\label{sec:asymptotic-learning}

Bayesian convergence does not imply identification: beliefs may stabilize at a random
limit without concentrating on the true type if different profiles generate
observationally equivalent signals \textcite{Doob1953Stochastic,Williams1991Martingales,
Schervish1995Theory}. This section establishes the conditions under which the public
history of the mechanism discriminates types in the limit, combining Bayesian coherence
of beliefs, statistical separation between signal laws, and the singularity criterion
of \textcite{Kakutani1948,BlackwellDubins1962}.

The proof strategy requires: \textbf{(a)} a well-defined public filtration on which
$\mu_t$ is a martingale \textup{(}Definition~\ref{def:zeta-filtration} and
Definition~\ref{def:beliefs-martingale}\textup{)}; \textbf{(b)} a long-run
identification condition on the prolonged captivity event $\mathcal{T}$, which prevents
the signal laws of different types from explaining the same observable trajectory
\textup{(}Definition~\ref{def:asymptotic-id}\textup{)}; and \textbf{(c)} uniform
total-variation separation provided by heterogeneity in $\beta_{K,j}$
\textup{(}Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}\textup{)}, which guarantees mutual
singularity of trajectory laws and the collapse of posterior masses on false types.

\subsection{Public Filtration, Likelihood, and the Role of IR/IC}
\label{subsec:P-IR-IC-convergence-mu}

Fixing the perfect Bayesian equilibrium of the mechanism, $\ProbE$ is the ex ante law
on the sample space $\Omega$ and describes the complete experiment before knowing the
captor type. First, after observing location $z$ and victim profile $\theta_V$, the type
is drawn according to the common structural prior, $\theta_K\sim\mu_0$, with
$\mu_0(\theta\mid z,\theta_V)=\ProbE(\theta_K=\theta\mid z,\theta_V)$. Then, conditional
on that type, the PBE protocol, public policy, executed actions, MDG filter, outcomes
$(m_t,d_t)$, and evolution of the public history $h_t$ unfold. Under $\ProbE$, the type
is ex ante random and the observable trajectory is interpreted as a \textbf{mixture} of
realizations compatible with different types, with initial marginal conditional on
$(z,\theta_V)$ given by $\mu_0(\theta\mid z,\theta_V)$.

\begin{definition}[Public signal and filtration]
\label{def:zeta-filtration}
\noindent\textbf{\textup{(i)} Public signal $\zeta_t$.}\quad
The \textbf{public signal} $\zeta_t$ is the random vector summarizing what is observable
at the close of period $t$. The order of its components reproduces the way in which the
public history incorporates, at the close of each period, the chosen instruments, the
executed actions, and the observed results: $\alpha_t\in[0,1]$ and $\gamma_t\in[0,1]$
are the State's continuous \textbf{instruments}; $(\tilde{a}_t^F,\tilde{a}_t^K,
\tilde{a}_t^S)$ are the \textbf{executed discrete actions} that appear as public
observables after the MDG process and do not replace equilibrium intentions
$a_t^{i\ast}$ in the record; $m_t\in\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{out}}$ is the
\textbf{physical outcome}; and $d_t\in\{0,1\}$ is the \textbf{detection signal}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\textup{(ii)} Value space.}\quad
$\zeta_t$ takes values in the product space
\[
\mathcal{Z}_t \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \times \mathcal{A}^F \times \mathcal{A}^K
\times \mathcal{A}^{S,\mathrm{disc}} \times \mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{out}} \times \{0,1\},
\]
where the discrete components are symbols in finite sets and the continuous components
are real coordinates. Thus, $\zeta_t$ codes what is observable at the close of $t$ and
summarizes the public innovation that feeds the next history of the episode.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\textup{(iii)} Public filtration
$\{\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\}_{t\ge0}$.}\quad
The initial instant is fixed by
\[
\mathcal{F}_0^{\mathrm{pub}}:=\sigma(z,\theta_V),
\]
that is, the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the observable geographic location at the
opening of the episode and the public victim profile. For each integer $t\ge1$,
\[
\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}:=\sigma(z,\theta_V,\zeta_0,\ldots,\zeta_{t-1}),
\]
that is, the $\sigma$-algebra generated by the initial location, the public victim
profile, and the signals observed \textbf{strictly before} period $t$ begins
\textup{(equivalently, ``at the opening of day $t$'' in the document's temporal
reading)}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{\textup{(iv)} Measurability.}\quad
Each $\zeta_t:(\Omega,\mathcal{F})\to(\mathcal{Z}_t,\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{Z}_t))$ is
assumed measurable, and $z$ and $\theta_V$ are observable at the opening of the episode,
so that $\sigma(z,\theta_V,\zeta_0,\ldots,\zeta_{t-1})$ is well-defined as a
sub-$\sigma$-algebra of $\mathcal{F}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Predictive likelihood under a counterfactual type]
\label{def:predictive-likelihood}
Given a perfect Bayesian equilibrium with profile $(\chi,s^F,s^K)$, the stochastic
dynamics of the mechanism, and a counterfactual type $\theta\in\Theta_K$, the
\textbf{predictive likelihood} of period $t$ is defined by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:likelihood-zeta}
\ell_t(\theta;J)\;:=\;\ProbC{\theta}\bigl(\zeta_t \in J \,\big|\, \mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\bigr),
\qquad J\in\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{Z}_t),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{Z}_t$ is the value space of the public signal and $J$ represents an
observable subset of that space. Conditioning is interpreted as the conditional
expectation of $\mathbf{1}\{\zeta_t\in J\}$ given the public information accumulated up
to $t$, defined up to $\ProbC{\theta}$-null sets through standard regular versions.
The likelihoods $\mathcal{L}_{F,t}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{C,t}$ are the operational
realization of this kernel for the physical block and the communication block of the
period.
\end{definition}

The architecture of the model postulates that the likelihood of observable
trajectories, denoted by
$\mathcal{L}_t(\theta)=\mathcal{L}_{F,t}(\theta)\mathcal{L}_{C,t}(\theta)$, is not a
statistically invariant object, but is subject to the \textbf{Critique of
\textcite{Lucas1976}}. With any modification in the mechanism $\chi$, whether through
the hardening of instruments or greater operational aggressiveness by the State
($a_t^S$), agents reoptimize their strategies in response to the new institutional
environment, thereby recomposing the equilibrium map $(\chi,s^F,s^K)$ and the
predictive likelihood of behaviors encoded in the informational flow $\zeta_t$. In this
framework, the conditional kernel $\ell_t$ acts as the formal bridge between the latent
state space ($\theta\in\Theta_K$) and the observable space ($\mathcal{Z}_t$). It is
essential to note that the \textbf{participation (IR)} and \textbf{incentive
compatibility (IC)} restrictions \textcite{Myerson1979IC,BoltonDewatripont2005} do not
replace this process, but \textbf{anchor} the functional form of $\ell_t$ by restricting
the set of admissible strategic profiles; only under a profile satisfying these
conditions does the observed likelihood coincide with that induced by an implementable
mechanism.

\subsection{Belief Martingale and Almost Sure Convergence}

The \textbf{martingale} structure of $\mu_t(\theta)$ under the ex ante law $\ProbE$
guarantees that each posterior mass may stabilize at a random limit $Z_\theta$ as
$t\to\infty$. Long-run identification does not create those limits; rather, it
determines their content under the law conditioned on the true type and on the
prolonged captivity event, making the masses on false types vanish and the remaining
mass concentrate on the realized type. Thus, two problems are separated: convergence of
beliefs as coherent sequential prediction, and collapse to the truth when the public
experiment discriminates types.

\subsection{Formalization of the Initial Belief: the Structural Prior}
\label{subsec:prior-estructural}

To guarantee the operational consistency of Bayesian learning, the State must start
from a grounded conjecture that collects the historical trajectory of the conflict.
Based on the empirical evidence, the initial belief $\mu_0$ is formalized through a
\textbf{Multinomial Logit (Softmax)} structure, which parametrizes the probability of
each type $\theta_K\in\Theta_K$ as a function of the observable attributes at the
opening of the episode:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:prior-initial-softmax}
\mu_0(\theta \mid z, \theta_V) =
\frac{\exp\left( \varpi_\theta + \eta_{\theta,z} + \xi_{\theta,V} \right)}
{\sum_{j \in \Theta_K} \exp\left( \varpi_j + \eta_{j,z} + \xi_{j,V} \right)}.
\end{equation}

This specification allows the initial probability mass to be filtered through three
structural dimensions linking the already defined types to the kidnapping environment.
First, \textbf{historical prevalence} ($\varpi_\theta$) acts as a base-frequency
intercept for each group $\theta\in\Theta_K$. Second, the \textbf{geographic
specialization filter} ($\eta_{\theta,z}$) modulates probability as a function of
location $z$: a metropolitan location increases belief mass toward common crime,
whereas presence in peripheral regions favors the identification of structures with
consolidated territorial control. Finally, the \textbf{visibility filter}
($\xi_{\theta,V}$) captures the strategic correlation between the victim profile
$\theta_V$ and the captor agenda, so the weight function tilts toward insurgent types
with political-pressure objectives whenever the victim has a public profile.

\begin{definition}[Belief process as martingale]
\label{def:beliefs-martingale}
For each type $\theta\in\Theta_K$, the posterior belief process is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:beliefs-ebp-consistency}
\mu_t(\theta)
=\ProbE\!\left(\theta_K=\theta\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\right),
\qquad t\ge0.
\end{equation}
The process $\{\mu_t(\theta)\}_{t\ge0}$ is adapted to
$\{\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\}_{t\ge0}$, bounded in $[0,1]$, integrable, and satisfies
the tower property
\[
\E^{\ProbE}\!\left[\mu_{t+1}(\theta)\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\right]
=\mu_t(\theta)
\qquad \ProbE\text{-a.s.}
\]
Thus, for every fixed $\theta$, $\{\mu_t(\theta)\}_{t\ge0}$ is a bounded martingale
under the ex ante law $\ProbE$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}{\textbf{Asymptotic stability of beliefs.} \textnormal{(Doob martingale convergence theorem)}}
\label{lem:asymptotic-beliefs}
Under the ex ante law $\ProbE$, the belief process $\{\mu_t(\theta)\}_{t\ge0}$ is a
\textbf{bounded martingale} that converges almost surely to a limit random variable
$Z_\theta$ with $Z_\theta\in[0,1]$ $\ProbE$-a.s.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:beliefs-martingale},
$\mu_t(\theta)=\ProbE(\theta_K=\theta\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}})$. The tower
property gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lem74-tower}
\E^{\ProbE}\!\left[\mu_{t+1}(\theta)\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}\right]
=\mu_t(\theta).
\end{equation}
Since $\mu_t(\theta)$ is a conditional probability,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lem74-pointwise-bound}
0\le \mu_t(\theta)\le1\qquad \ProbE\text{-a.s.}
\end{equation}
Thus the process is uniformly bounded in $L^1$. Doob's martingale convergence theorem
implies the existence of $Z_\theta$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lem74-as-limit}
\mu_t(\theta)\longrightarrow Z_\theta
\qquad \ProbE\text{-a.s.}
\end{equation}
and the bound passes to the limit, so $Z_\theta\in[0,1]$ almost surely.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Identification and Concentration on the True Type}
\label{subsec:identificacion-concentracion-tipo}

Lemma~\ref{lem:asymptotic-beliefs} guarantees that, under $\ProbE$, each belief process
$\mu_t(\theta)$ is a bounded martingale converging almost surely to a limit
$Z_\theta$. However, statistical convergence \emph{per se} does not imply that the
limit is binary ($Z_\theta\in\{0,1\}$). In Bayesian learning models, several types may
remain indistinguishable if the public signal structure does not allow them to be
discriminated in the limit \textcite{Schervish1995Theory}.

\begin{definition}[Prolonged captivity event]
\label{def:event-T-long}
Let $\mathcal{T}$ be the event in which the captivity episode remains active
indefinitely:
\[
\mathcal{T}:=\{\omega\in\Omega:\iota_t(\omega)=1\ \text{for all }t\ge0\}.
\]
Equivalently, $\mathcal{T}$ is the tail event in which no absorbing outcome terminates
the episode in finite time. It is the asymptotic regime under which the public
experiment supplies an unbounded sequence of signals.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Asymptotic identification on $\mathcal{T}$]
\label{def:asymptotic-id}
The mechanism is \textbf{asymptotically identifiable} on $\mathcal{T}$ if, for the true
type $\theta_K^\ast$ and every false type $\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast$, the laws induced by
the public signal process $\{\zeta_t\}_{t\ge0}$ under $\theta_K^\ast$ and under
$\theta$ are mutually singular when restricted to $\mathcal{T}$. Equivalently, there
exists a tail event $A_\theta$ such that
\[
\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}(A_\theta\mid\mathcal{T})=1,
\qquad
\ProbC{\theta}(A_\theta\mid\mathcal{T})=0.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Hazard separation and total variation]
\label{lem:hazard-separation}
Suppose there exist two distinct types $\theta\neq\theta'$ and a set of periods
$\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}\subseteq\mathbb{N}$ infinite on $\mathcal{T}$ such that
the competing-risk coefficients differ on at least one closing channel,
$\beta_{K,j}(\theta)\neq\beta_{K,j}(\theta')$ for some $j\in\{1,2,3\}$, and the policy
path keeps the corresponding channel active. Then there exists
$\underline{\varepsilon}_{\theta,\theta'}>0$ such that, for all
$t\in\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:TV-separation}
\left\|p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}})
-p_{\theta'}(\cdot\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}})\right\|_{\mathrm{TV}}
\ge \underline{\varepsilon}_{\theta,\theta'}.
\end{equation}
Consequently, the induced trajectory laws are mutually singular under the repeated
experiment on $\mathcal{T}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Differences in $\beta_{K,j}$ change the proportional hazards
$\lambda_j(t\mid\mathcal{C}_t,\theta)$ and therefore the one-period predictive
distribution of $Y_t=(m_t,d_t)$. When the channel remains active along infinitely many
periods, the one-step distributions remain separated in total variation by a positive
amount on an infinite subsequence. Coupling this recurrent separation with the product
experiment and Kakutani's criterion yields mutual singularity of the infinite
trajectory laws.
\end{proof}

\subsection{\textit{MDG Process}, Materialization of Actions, and the True-Types Theorem}
\label{subsec:mano-dios-asymptotic}

The theorem is stated over laws induced by the full PBE, including the
intention-to-execution layer. Conditioning on $\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}}$ means
conditioning on histories where the realized actions $\tilde{a}_s^i$ have already been
observed, not only the intended equilibrium actions $a_s^{i\ast}$. The convergence
$\mu_t(\theta_K^\ast)\to1$ on $\mathcal{T}$ is convergence of beliefs about the type
\textbf{after} each day's public actions have materialized.

\begin{theorem}[Identification of rational types in unknown environments]
\label{thm:posterior-consistency}
Suppose the mechanism is asymptotically identifiable on $\mathcal{T}$ under
Definition~\ref{def:asymptotic-id}, the prior has full support,
$\mu_0(\theta)>0$ for all $\theta\in\Theta_K$, and beliefs are updated consistently
with the PBE as in Definition~\ref{def:beliefs-martingale}. Then, on the event
$\mathcal{T}$,
\[
\mu_t(\theta_K^\ast)\longrightarrow 1
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mu_t(\theta)\longrightarrow0\quad\forall\,\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast,
\]
almost surely under the law of the true type restricted to $\mathcal{T}$. Equivalently,
$\mu_t$ converges weakly to the Dirac measure $\delta_{\theta_K^\ast}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:asymptotic-beliefs}, $\{\mu_t(\theta)\}_{t\ge0}$ is a bounded
martingale and converges almost surely to a limit $Z_\theta\in[0,1]$. Asymptotic
identification implies that for every false type $\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast$ there is a
tail event $A_\theta$ with probability one under the true type on $\mathcal{T}$ and
probability zero under $\theta$. The likelihood ratio of $\theta$ against
$\theta_K^\ast$ therefore converges to zero on $A_\theta$ by the standard Bayesian
consistency argument under mutual singularity \textcite{BlackwellDubins1962,
Schervish1995Theory}. Since the prior has full support,
\[
\mu_t(\theta)
=\frac{\mu_0(\theta)L_t(\theta)}
{\sum_{\theta'\in\Theta_K}\mu_0(\theta')L_t(\theta')}
\longrightarrow0
\qquad\forall\,\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast.
\]
Finiteness of $\Theta_K$ allows the convergence to hold simultaneously for all false
types. Since posterior masses sum to one, the remaining mass converges to the true type:
$\mu_t(\theta_K^\ast)\to1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Finite horizon and interpretation of the theorem]
\label{rmk:horizonte-finito-consistencia}
The theorem is an asymptotic identification result on the event $\mathcal{T}$. In a
finite episode, especially one that ends quickly, the mechanism may generate only a
finite number of observations; in that case the relevant conclusion is martingale
convergence and stabilization of beliefs, not necessarily point identification of the
true type. Point identification in short episodes would require repeated independent
episodes or an extension of the horizon.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Properties, Limitations, and Dynamics of Learning}
\label{subsec:learning-properties}

The posterior ratio between two types evolves according to the accumulation of log
likelihood ratios. For a true high-risk type and a low-risk alternative, the hazard
ratio
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hazard-ratio-tipo}
\frac{\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_{high})}{\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_{low})}
=\exp\!\left(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})-\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})\right)
\end{equation}
summarizes the structural wedge that feeds learning. When realized outcomes repeatedly
favor the high-risk law, the posterior tilts toward $\theta_{high}$; when the
likelihoods remain close, learning is slow or incomplete.

Under the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency}, this evidence flow is
not a random walk on the belief simplex but a systematic tilt toward
$\theta_K^\ast$. When the materialized action coincides with rational equilibrium, the
posterior concentrates through direct recognition of preferences and validation of IC.
When the \textit{MDG process} introduces a deviation, the Bayesian filter compares the
hazard structures compatible with the observed trajectory and assigns mass to the type
whose risk technology is most coherent with it.

Outside these limits, the mechanism transcends mere statistical convergence to reach
asymptotic identification. Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium guarantees, by
Lemma~\ref{lem:asymptotic-beliefs}, that $\mu_t$ converges to some random limit; but
only when Definition~\ref{def:asymptotic-id} is satisfied does that limit collapse to
the Dirac measure $\delta_{\theta_K^\ast}$.

\subsection{Equilibrium of the Mechanism, Integration of PBE and Implementability}
\label{subsec:equilibrio-mecanismo-introduccion}

The learning result must be read together with equilibrium existence and
implementability. Bayesian updating is meaningful only on histories generated by a
strategy profile that is sequentially rational and compatible with the informational
structure of the mechanism. For this reason, the next definitions state the PBE object
and the implementability property that connects the social choice rule to incentive and
participation constraints.

\begin{definition}[Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium of the mechanism]
\label{def:pbe-mechanism}
A \textbf{Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium} of the mechanism is a pair $(s,\mu)$, where
$s=(\chi,s^K,s^F)$ is a strategy profile for the State, the captor, and the family, and
$\mu=\{\mu_t\}_{t\ge0}$ is a system of beliefs, such that:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textup{(a)}] \textbf{Sequential rationality.} At every information set reached
  with positive probability, each agent's strategy is optimal given beliefs $\mu_t$ and
  the continuation strategies of the other agents.
  \item[\textup{(b)}] \textbf{Bayesian consistency.} At every reached node,
  \begin{equation}
  \label{eq:def712-bayes-mu}
  \mu_t(\theta)=\ProbE^{s}\!\left(\theta_K=\theta
  \mid\sigma(z,\theta_V,\zeta_0,\ldots,\zeta_{t-1})\right),
  \end{equation}
  and off-path beliefs are admissible and compatible with sequential rationality.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Implementable mechanism]
\label{def:implementable-mechanism}
Given a PBE $(s^\ast,\mu^\ast)$, the mechanism \textbf{implements} the social choice
rule $f_t$ if the equilibrium outcome induced by $s^\ast$ coincides with the State's
policy rule that minimizes expected social loss subject to incentive compatibility and
participation constraints. In particular, the equilibrium policy must satisfy the
Bayesian incentive compatibility constraint for the captor $IC^K$, the participation
constraint for the captor $IR^K$, and the participation constraint for the family
$IR^F$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Economic Reading of the Implementability Result}

\noindent The implementability analysis involves two logically distinct layers. The first,
formalized in Definition~\ref{def:pbe-mechanism}, is one of \textbf{strategic
consistency}: the existence of a profile $(s,\mu)$ in which the State, the captor, and
the family are sequentially rational given $\mu_t$, and in which beliefs $\mu_t$ are
consistent with Bayes' rule at reached nodes. The second, articulated in
Definition~\ref{def:implementable-mechanism}, is one of \textbf{implementability}: that
this same profile also satisfies $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$, so that the social choice
rule $f_t$, the rescue or negotiation policy plus instruments $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$
that minimize expected loss, is sustainable as an equilibrium \emph{outcome} without
any agent having an incentive to deviate. The PBE fixes who acts, with what beliefs, and
with what rationality; implementability determines whether the public \emph{outcome}
desired by the planner can be maintained when types are private and participation is
voluntary.

\medskip

\noindent Theorem~\ref{thm:ebp-implementabilidad} establishes that both layers are
compatible under verifiable conditions. The central hypothesis is \textbf{robust
feasibility}~\textup{(v)}: the non-emptiness of the set $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ of policies
simultaneously admissible under $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$ at each date and for each
belief. Since $\Gamma_t$ is compact, with continuous and convex sections in the
continuous instruments, and since the correspondence $\mu_t\mapsto\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ is
continuous, the State's restricted program admits a solution by the Weierstrass theorem
and the optimal correspondence is upper hemicontinuous. Quasiconvexity of the restricted
objective \textup{(hypothesis~\textup{(vii)})} makes that correspondence convex-valued,
which enables Kakutani's fixed point theorem \parencite{Kakutani1941} and guarantees the
existence of the PBE. Full support of the MDG process \textup{(hypothesis~\textup{(iii)})}
ensures that all relevant nodes are reached with positive probability, making Bayesian
updating operative on the path and reducing \emph{off-path} indeterminacy. The result
does not add a PBE to a previously designed mechanism: the social rule that minimizes
expected loss subject to $\Gamma_t$ emerges directly as an equilibrium trajectory.

\medskip

\noindent Theorem~\ref{thm:ebp-implementabilidad} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency} guarantee substantively distinct properties that
operate on complementary planes. The \textbf{existence of the PBE}
\textup{(enabled by hypotheses~\textup{(ii)}, \textup{(v)}, and~\textup{(vii)})} is a
necessary condition for coherence of the model as a game: without it there is no
equilibrium path on which to formulate optimal policy. \textbf{Nash--Bayes
implementability} with $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$ is the design condition: it delimits
the set of sustainable allocations when the captor's type is private information and
the family's participation cannot be forced. \textbf{Asymptotic learning}
\textup{(Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}, Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency})}
is a property of the informational flow: under uniform total-variation separation
between types, beliefs converge almost surely to the true type in the prolonged
captivity regime $\mathcal{T}$. These three properties are logically independent: a
mechanism may implement a social rule and nevertheless generate observationally
equivalent types in the short run; Bayesian convergence, in turn, neither implies nor
requires that the implemented policy be the one that minimizes expected loss.
Theorem~\ref{thm:ebp-implementabilidad} should therefore be read as a \emph{conditional}
guarantee on primitive feasibility~\textup{(v)}: it states that the policy defined by
the State's restricted program can be sustained as a PBE of the given mechanism, not
that every admissible policy is sustainable independently of the incentive structure.

\begin{theorem}[Conditional implementability of the mechanism in PBE]
\label{thm:ebp-implementabilidad}
Consider the mechanism of Definition~\ref{def:pbe-mechanism} under the following
conditions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item[\textup{(i)}] $|\Theta_K|<\infty$, with strictly positive prior
  $\mu_0(\theta)>0$ for every $\theta\in\Theta_K$.
  \item[\textup{(ii)}] The action spaces $\mathcal{A}^i$ are finite for every
  $i\in\{K,S,F\}$ and the game tree has a finite number of nodes in each period $t$, so
  that each agent's behavioral strategy space is a finite product of simplexes.
  \item[\textup{(iii)}] The implementation law $\ProbI{i}$ of the MDG process assigns
  strictly positive probability to every action $a\in\mathcal{A}^i$ for every plan
  $a_t^{i\ast}$ and state $X_t$ \textup{(full support, \eqref{eq:mano-dios-jugador})}.
  \item[\textup{(iv)}] The instrument sets are $\mathcal{A}^{\alpha}=[0,1]$ and
  $\mathcal{A}^{\gamma}=[0,1]$, compact and convex; the functions $L_t$, $G$, and
  $\mathcal{U}_t^i$ are measurable, uniformly bounded in $\mathbb{T}$, and continuous
  in the continuous decision variables.
  \item[\textup{(v)}] \textbf{Robust feasibility.} For each $t$ and each
  $\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$, the feasible set $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ defined in
  \eqref{eq:gamma-factible} is non-empty and compact. For each discrete branch
  $a_t^S$, its continuous section
  \[
  \Gamma_t^{a^S}(\mu_t):=\{(\alpha_t,\gamma_t):(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\Gamma_t(\mu_t)\}
  \]
  is convex, and the correspondence $\mu_t\mapsto\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ is
  \textbf{continuous} \textup{(upper and lower hemicontinuous)} and compact-valued on
  $\Delta(\Theta_K)$.\footnote{This is the standard framework for applying Berge's
  maximum theorem to the restricted policy; see \textcite{AliprantisBorder2006}.}
  Non-emptiness is a primitive condition of simultaneous compatibility between
  $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$.
  \item[\textup{(vi)}] \textbf{Parametric separability.} For every $t\in\mathbb{T}$,
  the captor's value function admits the representation
  \[
    V^K_t\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta')\mid\theta_K,\alpha_t,\gamma_t\bigr)
    =
    \sum_{j}\,w_j\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta'),\alpha_t,\gamma_t\bigr)
    \cdot\exp\!\bigl(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_K)\cdot z\bigr),
  \]
  where the weights $w_j(\cdot)$ depend on the action path $a^\ast(\theta')$ and on the
  instruments, but not on $\theta_K$. This condition makes it possible to audit the
  economic direction of incentives; it does not replace the verification of $IC^K$ in
  \textup{(v)} nor the statistical identification of Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}.
  \item[\textup{(vii)}] \textbf{Quasiconvexity of the restricted objective.} For each
  $t$, each $\mu_t\in\Delta(\Theta_K)$, and each discrete branch
  $a_t^S\in\mathcal{A}^{S,\mathrm{disc}}$, the functional
  \[
  (\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\;\mapsto\;\sum_{\theta_K\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta_K)\,L_t(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\theta_K,\iota_t)
  +\Pi_{t,a^S}^S(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t)
  -\psi_H\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
  \]
  is quasiconvex in $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$ over $\Gamma_t^{a^S}(\mu_t)$. Combined with
  the convexity of $\Gamma_t^{a^S}(\mu_t)$ \textup{(hypothesis~\textup{(v)})}, this
  condition guarantees that the restricted optimum correspondence
  $\Psi_t(\mu_t):=\arg\min_{\Gamma_t^{a^S}(\mu_t)}\{\cdot\}$ has convex values, making
  Kakutani's fixed point theorem applicable to the State's program.
\end{itemize}

Then there exists a pair $(s^\ast,\mu^\ast)$, with
$s^\ast=({\chi}^\ast,s^{K\ast},s^{F\ast})$, that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[\textup{(a)}] Constitutes a \textbf{Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium} in the
    sense of Definition~\ref{def:pbe-mechanism}: strategies in $s^\ast$ are mutual best
    responses given $\mu^\ast$, and beliefs $\mu_t^\ast$ are Bayes-consistent at nodes
    reached with positive probability.
    \item[\textup{(b)}] \textbf{Implements the social choice rule} $f_t$: on the
    equilibrium path, $\chi_t^\ast(h_t)=(a_t^{S\ast},\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)$
    coincides with the allocation that minimizes expected social loss
    \eqref{eq:state-expected-loss} subject to $\Gamma_t(\mu_t^\ast)$, according to rule
    \eqref{eq:state-discrete-rule}.
    \item[\textup{(c)}] \textbf{Satisfies $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$} in the profile
    $(s^\ast,\mu^\ast)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in five steps: existence of the fixed point in the restricted game,
Bayesian consistency on the path, implementation of the social rule, incentive
compatibility, and participation constraints.

\paragraph{1. Existence of equilibrium.}
By hypothesis~(ii) and the finite horizon of Definition~\ref{def:pbe-mechanism}, each
agent's behavioral strategy space $\Sigma^i$ is a finite product of simplexes, compact
and convex. The State's restriction is formulated through the feasible correspondence
$\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$: by hypothesis~(v), $\Gamma_t$ is compact and non-empty, with
continuous convex sections in each discrete branch and \textbf{continuous} in $\mu_t$.

Given a profile $s$, the on-path beliefs $\mu_t(s)$ are measurable at reached nodes. The
State's program over $(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\in\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ has a continuous
objective in $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$ by hypothesis~(iv) and a compact feasible set; by the
maximum theorem \textup{(Berge; see \textcite{AliprantisBorder2006})}, the optimal value
is continuous in $\mu_t$ and the correspondence of optimal sets is upper
hemicontinuous and compact. By hypothesis~(vii), the objective functional is
quasiconvex in $(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)$ over each convex section
$\Gamma_t^{a^S}(\mu_t)$; hence the argmin over a non-empty compact convex set is convex,
so the planner's best-response correspondence in mixed strategies has convex values.

The expected utilities of $K$ and $F$ are multilinear in behavioral strategies.
Together, the restricted best-response correspondence
\[
\mathrm{BR}(s)\;:=\;\mathrm{BR}^S_\Gamma(s^{-S})\times\mathrm{BR}^K(s^{-K})\times\mathrm{BR}^F(s^{-F})
\]
is upper hemicontinuous, with non-empty and convex values over $\Sigma$
\textcite{FudenbergTirole1991}. By Kakutani's fixed point theorem
\textcite{Kakutani1941}, there exists $s^\ast$ such that $s^\ast\in\mathrm{BR}(s^\ast)$.
Hypothesis~(v) explicitly carries the simultaneous compatibility between $IC^K$,
$IR^K$, and $IR^F$; for this reason the result is conditional and not an unconditional
proof of non-emptiness of $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$.

\paragraph{2. Bayesian consistency on the path.}
Hypothesis~(iii) implies that
$\ProbI{i}(\tilde{a}_t^i=a\mid a_t^{i\ast},X_t)>0$ for every
$a\in\mathcal{A}^i$, so the ex ante law $\ProbE^{s^\ast}$ assigns strictly positive
probability to realized actions compatible with the physical dynamics and with nodes
reachable under the public history. At those nodes, Bayes' formula
\[
\mu_t^\ast(\theta)\;=\;\ProbE^{s^\ast}\!\bigl(\theta_K=\theta\mid\sigma(z,\theta_V,\zeta_0,\ldots,\zeta_{t-1})\bigr)
\]
is well defined and satisfies condition~(b) of Definition~\ref{def:pbe-mechanism}. For
off-path nodes, beliefs are taken to be admissible and compatible with sequential
rationality in the remaining subgames. The pair $(s^\ast,\mu^\ast)$ thus constructed is
a PBE in the sense of \textcite{FudenbergTirole1991,KrepsWilson1982}; uniqueness of
off-path beliefs is not asserted.

\paragraph{3. Implementation of the social choice rule.}
At the fixed point, $S$'s strategy solves at every on-path node the restricted program
\[
(a_t^{S\ast},\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast)\;\in\;
\arg\min_{(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t)\,\in\,\Gamma_t(\mu_t^\ast)}
\left\{
\sum_{\theta_K\in\Theta_K}\mu_t^\ast(\theta_K)\,L_t(a_t^S,\alpha_t,\gamma_t,\theta_K,\iota_t)
+\Pi_{t,a^S}^S(\alpha_t,\gamma_t;\mu_t^\ast)
-\psi_H\Delta H_t(\alpha_t,\gamma_t)
\right\}.
\]
This solution coincides with program~\eqref{eq:state-expected-loss} subject to IR/IC
and rule~\eqref{eq:state-discrete-rule}; defining
$f_t(\mathcal{I}_t^S):=\chi_t^\ast(h_t)$, the equilibrium policy implements the social
choice rule on the path.

\paragraph{4. Incentive compatibility \textup{(}$IC^K$\textup{)}: reformulation by cycles \parencite{Rochet1987}.}
By Step~3, the equilibrium policy belongs to $\Gamma_t(\mu_t^\ast)$ at every on-path
node. By hypothesis~\textup{(v)} and Definition~\ref{def:implementable-mechanism},
$\Gamma_t(\mu_t^\ast)$ incorporates $IC^K$ as a \textbf{constitutive} condition: for
every rival imitation $\theta_j\in\Theta_K$,
\[
\mathbb{E}_{\theta\sim\mu_t^\ast}
\left[
V^K_t\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta)\mid\theta,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast\bigr)
-
V^K_t\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta_j)\mid\theta,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast\bigr)
\right]
\ge0.
\]
Thus $IC^K$ is established directly by membership in $\Gamma_t$, without appealing to
the State's loss-minimization program~\eqref{eq:state-expected-loss}.

For an equivalent algebraic check in the finite menu, let
$\Theta_K=\{\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n\}$ and define
\[
\Delta_{ij}^t
\;:=\;
V^K_t\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta_j)\mid\theta_i,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast\bigr)
-
V^K_t\!\bigl(a^\ast(\theta_i)\mid\theta_i,\alpha_t^\ast,\gamma_t^\ast\bigr).
\]
The Bayesian version of $IC^K$ is equivalent to requiring
\[
\mathbb{E}_{\theta_i\sim\mu_t^\ast}\!\left[\Delta_{ij}^t\right]\le0
\qquad \forall j=1,\ldots,n.
\]
In particular, for every cycle $(\theta_{k_0},\theta_{k_1},\ldots,\theta_{k_m},\theta_{k_0})$
in $\Theta_K$, expected verification takes the finite form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:rochet-cycle}
\sum_{\ell=0}^{m}
\mu_t^\ast(\theta_{k_\ell})\,
\Delta_{k_\ell,\,k_{\ell+1}}^t
\;\le\;0
\qquad\text{(indices mod }m+1\text{)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{5. Participation constraints \textup{(}$IR^K$, $IR^F$\textup{)}.}
By construction in Step~3, the equilibrium instruments belong to
$\Gamma_t(\mu_t^\ast)$, a set that by hypothesis~(v) simultaneously guarantees
$IR^K$~\eqref{eq:ir-K} and $IR^F$~\eqref{eq:ir-family} in the equilibrium profile.
Having completed the five steps, $(s^\ast,\mu^\ast)$ is a PBE that implements $f_t$ and
satisfies $IC^K$, $IR^K$, and $IR^F$ simultaneously.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Separation of results: implementability and asymptotic identification]
The present theorem and Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency} establish distinct
properties that complement one another. Theorem~\ref{thm:ebp-implementabilidad}
guarantees, under robust feasibility~\textup{(v)}, that the mechanism has a PBE in
which the equilibrium policy implements the social choice rule; $IC^K$ enters through
the definition of $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$ and Definition~\ref{def:implementable-mechanism},
and the cyclic form~\eqref{eq:rochet-cycle} is a verifiable reformulation when grouping
pairs is convenient. The dynamic revelation principle of \textcite{PavanSegalToikka2014}
typically assumes an ordered continuum $\Theta_K\subseteq\mathbb{R}$. Parametric
separability~\textup{(vi)} helps interpret and audit incentives from $\beta_{K,j}$, but
does not replace~\textup{(v)}. Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency}, in turn,
guarantees that \emph{under that same equilibrium}, if heterogeneity in $\beta_{K,j}$
generates uniform total-variation separation between types
(Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}), then beliefs converge almost surely to the true
type $\theta_K^\ast$ in regime $\mathcal{T}$.
\end{remark}

\section{Policy Implications}
\label{sec:policy-implications}

The results of the preceding sections have direct consequences for the design of
antikidnapping policy. Proposition~\ref{prop:expected-captivity-gamma} establishes that
the effect of $\gamma_t$ on expected captivity duration is governed by the sign of
$\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)$: only when pressure accelerates closure outcomes more than it
inhibits the payment channel is the episode shortened; otherwise, the State may
paradoxically prolong it. Proposition~\ref{prop:optimal-gamma-beliefs} translates this
heterogeneity into a Bayesian calibration rule: the State uses Bayes updating to
discipline its policy, so as evidence $(\mathcal{I}_t)$ increases weight on the
high-risk type, the planner intensifies $\gamma_t^\ast$ to exploit that sensitivity and
force early closure. The magnitude of the response is proportional to the structural
separation $\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})-\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})$, which acts as a
policy amplifier when new information arrives.

\begin{proposition}[Optimal operational pressure and Bayesian type identification]
\label{prop:optimal-gamma-beliefs}
Suppose $\Theta_K=\{\theta_{low},\theta_{high}\}$ with
$\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})>\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})$ for $j\in\{2,3\}$
\textup{(the high-risk type exhibits greater propensity to rescue and death)}, and that
the State's objective~\eqref{eq:state-expected-loss} is strictly convex in $\gamma_t$ in
the interior of $\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$. Then optimal operational pressure $\gamma_t^\ast$ is
strictly increasing in the posterior belief on the high-risk type:
\[
\frac{d\gamma_t^\ast}{d\mu_t(\theta_{high})} > 0.
\]
The elasticity of $\gamma_t^\ast$ with respect to $\mu_t(\theta_{high})$ depends on the
differential ratio of marginal losses between types, which in turn is proportional to
\[
\frac{\tilde\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_{high})}{\tilde\lambda_j(t\mid\theta_{low})}
= \exp\!\bigl(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})-\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})\bigr), \quad j\in\{2,3\}.
\]
Consequently, the range of variation of optimal pressure in response to Bayesian
updates is larger the more separated the structural parameters $\beta_{K,j}$ are across
types.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The proof proceeds in four steps: construction of the State's program, first-order
condition, application of the implicit function theorem, and derivation of structural
elasticity.

\paragraph{Step 1. State program and interior condition.}
With $\Theta_K=\{\theta_{low},\theta_{high}\}$ and
$\mu_t(\theta_{low})=1-\mu_t(\theta_{high})$, the State objective with respect to
$\gamma_t$, fixing $\mu_t$ and the remaining instruments, reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:obj-gamma}
\mathcal{W}(\gamma_t,\mu_t)
:= \mu_t(\theta_{high})\,L_t(\gamma_t,\theta_{high})
 + \bigl(1-\mu_t(\theta_{high})\bigr)\,L_t(\gamma_t,\theta_{low})
 + \chi_\gamma\bigl(\gamma_t-\gamma_t^\mu\bigr)^2.
\end{equation}
By the strict convexity hypothesis in $\gamma_t$ over the interior of
$\Gamma_t(\mu_t)$, $\mathcal{W}$ has a unique interior minimizer
$\gamma_t^\ast(\mu_t)$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:foc-gamma}
\Phi(\gamma_t,\mu_t)
:= \mu_t(\theta_{high})\,\frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{high})
 + \bigl(1-\mu_t(\theta_{high})\bigr)\,
   \frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{low})
 + 2\chi_\gamma\bigl(\gamma_t-\gamma_t^\mu\bigr)
= 0,
\end{equation}
with $\partial\Phi/\partial\gamma_t = \partial^2\mathcal{W}/\partial\gamma_t^2 > 0$.

\paragraph{Step 2. Implicit function theorem.}
Since $\Phi(\gamma_t^\ast,\mu_t)=0$ and $\partial\Phi/\partial\gamma_t>0$, the implicit
function theorem guarantees that $\gamma_t^\ast$ is continuously differentiable in
$\mu_t(\theta_{high})$ around the optimum, with derivative
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tfi}
\frac{d\gamma_t^\ast}{d\mu_t(\theta_{high})}
= -\frac{\partial\Phi/\partial\mu_t(\theta_{high})}
        {\partial\Phi/\partial\gamma_t}
= -\frac{
    \dfrac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{high})
    -\dfrac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{low})
  }{
    \partial\Phi/\partial\gamma_t
  }.
\end{equation}
Because the denominator is strictly positive, the sign of~\eqref{eq:tfi} is determined
by the sign of the marginal-loss difference in the numerator.

\paragraph{Step 3. Sign of the marginal-loss difference.}
Under proportional risks, $L_t(\gamma_t,\theta_K)$ depends on $\gamma_t$ through the
effective intensities
$\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_K)=\lambda_{j0}(t)
\exp\!\bigl(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_K)\cdot z+\zeta_{\gamma,j}\,\gamma_t\bigr)$.
Differentiating and forming the difference between types,
\begin{align}
\label{eq:diff-dL}
\frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{high})
-\frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\gamma_t}(\theta_{low})
&= \sum_{j\in\{2,3\}}\frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\tilde{\lambda}_j}\,
   \zeta_{\gamma,j}\,
   \Bigl[\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{high})
         -\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{low})\Bigr] \notag\\
&\quad
 + \frac{\partial L_t}{\partial\tilde{\lambda}_1}\,\zeta_{\gamma,1}\,
   \Bigl[\tilde{\lambda}_1(t\mid\theta_{high})
         -\tilde{\lambda}_1(t\mid\theta_{low})\Bigr].
\end{align}
The hazard ratio between types for $j\in\{2,3\}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:hazard-ratio}
\frac{\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{high})}
     {\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{low})}
= \exp\!\Bigl(\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})-\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})\Bigr)
> 1,
\end{equation}
so $\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{high})>\tilde{\lambda}_j(t\mid\theta_{low})$ for
$j\in\{2,3\}$. Since $\partial L_t/\partial\tilde{\lambda}_j<0$ for $j\in\{2,3\}$ and
$\zeta_{\gamma,j}>0$, the first term of~\eqref{eq:diff-dL} is strictly negative. By the
hypothesis $\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})>\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})$, this differential
dominates the effect of the payment channel ($j=1$), so the difference in
\eqref{eq:diff-dL} is negative.

\paragraph{Step 4. Conclusion and structural elasticity.}
Substituting in~\eqref{eq:tfi}, the numerator is negative and the denominator positive;
therefore $d\gamma_t^\ast/d\mu_t(\theta_{high})>0$. The magnitude of this adjustment is
proportional to the separation between high-risk and low-risk intensities, quantified by
ratio~\eqref{eq:hazard-ratio}: the larger
$\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{high})-\beta_{K,j}(\theta_{low})$, the larger the difference in
$\partial L_t/\partial\gamma_t$ between types and, therefore, the larger the optimal
response of $\gamma_t^\ast$ to Bayesian updates.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Policy Recommendations}

\paragraph{Integrate macrofinancial alerts into operational planning.}
Cointegration between ransom kidnapping, coercive capacity, and financial conditions
indicates that changes in liquidity and the cost of capital may modify the criminal
portfolio. Early-warning systems of GAULA units should incorporate regional indicators
of credit, liquidity, and financial pressure to anticipate changes in the expected
duration and profitability of kidnapping.

\paragraph{Apply financial interdiction adaptively.}
The block $\alpha_t^\ast$ disciplines captor participation and reduces the space for
clandestine agreements, but indiscriminate application may impose unnecessary costs on
the family. The severity of interdiction should respond to the posterior type, victim
profile, and family wealth. When the posterior concentrates and the marginal value of
experimentation falls, the block can be moderated without abandoning financial
traceability or deterrent capacity.

\paragraph{Adopt intelligence protocols based on Bayesian dual control.}
Operational pressure $\gamma_t^\ast$ should simultaneously pursue safe closure of the
episode and likelihood separation between organizations. The systematic combination of
detection signals, proofs of survival, captivity duration, and competing-risk patterns
makes it possible to classify the captor early, allocate tactical resources better, and
reduce the human risk of operations.

\section{Structural Identification: Singularity of Measures}
\label{app:general-id}

This section presents, in an abstract framework consistent with
Section~\ref{sec:asymptotic-learning} and Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}, why
\textbf{recurrent local separation} in total variation between daily laws couples with
the criterion of \textcite{Kakutani1948} to obtain \textbf{mutual singularity} of
trajectory laws. The prolonged captivity event $\mathcal{T}$
\textup{(Definition~\ref{def:event-T-long})} is a subset of $\Omega$; time indices are
$t\in\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Notation \textup{(}aligned with the body of the text\textup{)}.}
Given a policy path $\chi$ compatible with the PBE, let $Y_t=(m_t,d_t)$ be the daily
public record and let $p_\theta^{(t)}$ be the mass function of $Y_t$ conditional on the
public information at the opening of $t$ under type $\theta$ \textup{(same logic as
$p_\theta(\cdot\mid\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}})$ in
Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation})}. For two masses $p,q$ on the same finite support,
the \textbf{affinity} \textup{(Bhattacharyya coefficient)} is
$\rho(p,q):=\sum_y \sqrt{p(y)q(y)}$; squared Hellinger distance adopts here the
normalization
\[
\mathsf{H}^2(p,q):=\frac{1}{2}\sum_y\bigl(\sqrt{p(y)}-\sqrt{q(y)}\bigr)^2 = 1-\rho(p,q).
\]
Total variation distance satisfies
$\|p-q\|_{\mathrm{TV}}=\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_y|p(y)-q(y)|$ \textup{(as in
\eqref{eq:TV-separation})}.

\begin{lemma}[Product of experiments and singularity \textup{(}Kakutani criterion\textup{)}]
\label{lem:general-id-appendix}
Fix two types $\theta\neq\theta'$, and suppose that, conditional on the policy fixed in
equilibrium and on the relevant comparison regime \textup{(in particular, on event
$\mathcal{T}$ when working with prolonged captivity)}, the sequence of daily experiments
$\{Y_t\}_{t\ge 0}$ is independent and each $Y_t$ takes values in a common finite support
$\mathcal{Y}_t$. That is, for each finite horizon $T$, the law of the trajectory
$(Y_0,\ldots,Y_T)$ factorizes as the product measure $\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T}
p_\theta^{(t)}$ under type $\theta$, and analogously as $\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T}
p_{\theta'}^{(t)}$ under $\theta'$. Let
$\rho_t:=\rho\bigl(p_\theta^{(t)},p_{\theta'}^{(t)}\bigr)$. Then the affinity between
the product laws at horizon $T$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:appendix-rho-product}
\rho\Bigl(\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T} p_\theta^{(t)},\,\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T} p_{\theta'}^{(t)}\Bigr)=\prod_{t=0}^{T}\rho_t.
\end{equation}
If there exists an infinite set of periods
$\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}\subseteq\mathbb{N}$ such that
$\|p_\theta^{(t)}-p_{\theta'}^{(t)}\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\ge \underline{\varepsilon}>0$ for
all $t\in\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}$, then
$\rho_t\le 1-\underline{\varepsilon}^{\,2}/2$ for all such $t$, and therefore
$\prod_{t\in\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}}\rho_t=0$. In the limit $T\to\infty$, the
criterion of \textcite{Kakutani1948} implies that the product measures over the infinite
sequence are \textbf{mutually singular} \textcite{Shiryaev2019Probability}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Identity~\eqref{eq:appendix-rho-product} is obtained by expanding the masses of finite
trajectories. Under independence,
\[
\rho\Bigl(\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T} p_\theta^{(t)},\,\bigotimes_{t=0}^{T} p_{\theta'}^{(t)}\Bigr)
=\sum_{(y_0,\ldots,y_T)}\prod_{t=0}^{T}\sqrt{p_\theta^{(t)}(y_t)p_{\theta'}^{(t)}(y_t)}
=\prod_{t=0}^{T}\sum_{y_t\in\mathcal{Y}_t}\sqrt{p_\theta^{(t)}(y_t)p_{\theta'}^{(t)}(y_t)}.
\]
For the second part, use the standard inequality
$\|p-q\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\le \sqrt{2}\,\mathsf{H}(p,q)$ in finite spaces. Since
$\mathsf{H}^2(p,q)=1-\rho(p,q)$, if
$\|p-q\|_{\mathrm{TV}}\ge\underline{\varepsilon}$, then
\[
\rho(p,q)\le 1-\frac{\underline{\varepsilon}^{\,2}}{2}.
\]
Thus, for each $t\in\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}$,
$\rho_t\le 1-\underline{\varepsilon}^{\,2}/2<1$. Since there are infinitely many
periods in $\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}$, the product of affinities contains
infinitely many factors uniformly bounded below one; hence
$\prod_{t\in\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta'}}\rho_t=0$. By the criterion of
\textcite{Kakutani1948} for products of probabilities, the associated infinite product
measures are mutually singular \textcite{Shiryaev2019Probability}.
\end{proof}

\medskip
\paragraph{General version of Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency}.}
Lemma~\ref{lem:general-id-appendix} isolates the \emph{pure} measure-theoretic argument:
under product structure and recurrent total-variation separation, the criterion of
\textcite{Kakutani1948} forces \textbf{mutual singularity} of trajectory laws. Theorem
~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency} in the body of the text invokes asymptotic
identification \textup{(Definition~\ref{def:asymptotic-id})} and, as a sufficient
verifiable route, Lemma~\ref{lem:hazard-separation}. The following result states the
same Bayesian conclusion replacing that route with the hypotheses of
Lemma~\ref{lem:general-id-appendix} on the daily experiment $Y_t$.

\begin{theorem}[Posterior consistency under singularity of product measures \textup{(}Kakutani criterion\textup{)}]
\label{thm:posterior-consistency-appendix}
Suppose $\Theta_K$ is finite, the prior $\mu_0$ has full support, and Bayesian updating
is consistent with the PBE \textup{(Definition~\ref{def:beliefs-martingale})}. Let
$\theta_K^\ast$ be the realized type and $\mathcal{T}$ the prolonged captivity event
\textup{(Definition~\ref{def:event-T-long})}. Suppose that, for every
$\theta\in\Theta_K$ with $\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast$, the following conditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item[\textup{(i)}] \textbf{Trajectory compatibility.}
  $\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}(\mathcal{T})>0$ and, for each false type, either
  $\ProbC{\theta}(\mathcal{T})=0$ \textup{(exclusion by survival)}, or the laws of
  signals are compared under the law conditioned/restricted to $\mathcal{T}$ as in the
  following clauses.
  \item[\textup{(ii)}] \textbf{Product and recurrent TV separation.} For every false
  type with $\ProbC{\theta}(\mathcal{T})>0$, conditional on the policy fixed by
  equilibrium and on event $\mathcal{T}$, the sequence $\{Y_t\}_{t\ge 0}$ satisfies the
  product factorization and the existence of $\mathcal{S}_{\theta,\theta_K^\ast}$ and
  $\underline{\varepsilon}_\theta>0$ described in
  Lemma~\ref{lem:general-id-appendix}.
  \item[\textup{(iii)}] \textbf{Signal--experiment alignment.} In $\mathcal{T}$, the
  public signal component $\zeta_t$ contains $Y_t=(m_t,d_t)$; since $Y_t$ is
  $\sigma(\zeta_t)$-measurable, mutual singularity of the laws of
  $\{Y_t\}_{t\ge0}$ transfers to that of $\{\zeta_t\}_{t\ge0}$ in the sense of
  part~\textup{(b)} of Definition~\ref{def:asymptotic-id}.
\end{enumerate}
Then, in regime $\mathcal{T}$,
\[
\mu_t(\theta)\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}0
\qquad
\forall\,\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast,
\qquad
\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}\textup{-a.s. in }\mathcal{T},
\]
and
\[
\mu_t(\theta_K^\ast)\xrightarrow[t\to\infty]{}1
\qquad \ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}\textup{-a.s. in }\mathcal{T}.
\]
Equivalently, in $\mathcal{T}$, $\{\mu_t\}$ converges weakly,
$\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}$-a.s., to $\delta_{\theta_K^\ast}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix $\theta\neq\theta_K^\ast$. First we prove that the posterior of the false type
converges to zero. If $\ProbC{\theta}(\mathcal{T})=0$, the comparison regime
$\mathcal{T}$ does not occur under the false type, while
$\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}(\mathcal{T})>0$ by \textup{(i)}. Therefore, under the true law
restricted to $\mathcal{T}$, the false type is ruled out by differential survival and
its posterior likelihood vanishes.

Now suppose $\ProbC{\theta}(\mathcal{T})>0$. By \textup{(ii)}, the laws of
$\{Y_t\}_{t\ge0}$ factorize as products and exhibit recurrent total-variation
separation. Lemma~\ref{lem:general-id-appendix} implies that the infinite product laws
induced by $\theta$ and by $\theta_K^\ast$ are mutually singular. By \textup{(iii)}, that
singularity transfers to the public flow $\{\zeta_t\}_{t\ge0}$ feeding Bayesian
updating. Thus, there exists a tail event $A_\theta$ such that
\[
\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}(A_\theta\mid\mathcal{T})=1,
\qquad
\ProbC{\theta}(A_\theta\mid\mathcal{T})=0.
\]
Let
\[
R_t^\theta
:=
\frac{d\,\ProbC{\theta}\!\mid_{\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}},\,\mathcal{T}}}
     {d\,\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}\!\mid_{\mathcal{F}_t^{\mathrm{pub}},\,\mathcal{T}}}
\]
be the finite likelihood ratio of the false type against the true type, defined with
respect to the Bayesian mixture with full-support prior. Tail singularity forces
$R_t^\theta\to0$ $\ProbC{\theta_K^\ast}$-a.s. in $\mathcal{T}$
\textcite{BlackwellDubins1962,Schervish1995Theory}. By Bayes,
\[
\mu_t(\theta)
=
\frac{\mu_0(\theta)R_t^\theta}
{\mu_0(\theta_K^\ast)+\sum_{\theta'\neq\theta_K^\ast}\mu_0(\theta')R_t^{\theta'}}
\le
\frac{\mu_0(\theta)}{\mu_0(\theta_K^\ast)}R_t^\theta,
\]
and since $\mu_0(\theta_K^\ast)>0$, $\mu_t(\theta)\to0$ almost surely in
$\mathcal{T}$. Since $\Theta_K$ is finite, all false coordinates converge to zero
simultaneously outside a finite null set. Finally, $\sum_{\theta\in\Theta_K}\mu_t(\theta)=1$
for every $t$, so passing to the limit in the finite sum gives
$\mu_t(\theta_K^\ast)\to1$. In a finite simplex, coordinate convergence to
$\delta_{\theta_K^\ast}$ is equivalent to weak convergence.
\end{proof}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presents a dynamic deterrence mechanism under incomplete information for the
analysis of ransom kidnapping, whose central contribution is the
\textbf{Theorem on the Identification of Rational Types in Unknown Environments}
(Theorem~\ref{thm:posterior-consistency}). The result establishes that, when public
policy generates asymptotically separable risk intensities across captor types, the
accumulation of daily signals forces almost sure convergence of the State's posterior
beliefs toward the true type $\theta_K^\ast$ in the prolonged captivity regime
$\mathcal{T}$.

Three structural elements support this result. First, the MDG process (Mano de
Dios--Guadalupe), axiomatically justified as an implementation law compatible with full
support, dominance, and maximum entropy (Remark~\ref{rem:axiomas-logit}), ensures that
the public history remains informative even off the equilibrium path. Second, the
Implementability Theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:ebp-implementabilidad}) establishes that
there exists a Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium implementing the social choice rule under
incentive compatibility $IC^K$, individual rationality $IR^K$, and $IR^F$. Third, the
structural identification section (Section~\ref{app:general-id}) provides the
measure-theoretic foundation: mutual singularity of trajectory laws, through the
Kakutani criterion, converts local statistical separation into global asymptotic
identification.

On the empirical plane, cointegration between kidnapping modalities and financial
conditions reveals a persistent environment that conditions retention costs. At the
microeconomic level, multinomial and competing-risk models show systematic
heterogeneity by perpetrator, victim, region, duration, and institutional presence.
These results turn the Colombian evidence into priors, intensities, and constraints
directly usable by the mechanism.

The dynamic calibration illustrates its operational viability. The optimal policy
combines financial interdiction, operational pressure, and informational value to
accelerate posterior concentration without abandoning incentive and participation
constraints. The policy implications (Section~\ref{sec:policy-implications}) are
therefore operational: $\gamma_t^\ast$ must be calibrated according to posterior beliefs
and structural separation between types, while its effect on duration depends on the net
sensitivity $\kappa_h(\theta_K,t)$ of competing risks.

The future agenda includes extension to continuous types
$\Theta_K\subseteq\mathbb{R}$, endogenous strategic communication between family and
State, structures with multiple private agents, and structural estimation with panel
data on kidnapping episodes. These extensions would make it possible to transfer the
framework to other asymmetric-information problems, such as financial supervision,
labor selection, and surveillance of illicit organizations.
